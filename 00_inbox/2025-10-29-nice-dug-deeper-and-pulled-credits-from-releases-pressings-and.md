# Nice — dug deeper and pulled credits from releases, pressings and…

Nice — dug deeper and pulled credits from releases, pressings and interviews. Short answer up front: Ivy Lab usually master with (and for many releases credit) Beau Thomas, while some recent pressings/versions of Infinite Falling Ground Pt.2 credit Bob Macc. Mixing credits are far less consistent in public listings — a lot of their releases don’t list a separate mix engineer, which usually means the duo (Sabre & Stray / Ivy Lab) do much of the producing/mixing themselves.
What I found (concrete credits)
Beau Thomas — credited as Mastered By on multiple Ivy Lab releases (example: 20/20 Volume One, Fortuna EP, several EP/pressing credits and lacquers). (Discogs)
Bob Macc — appears as Mastered By on at least one Discogs entry for Infinite Falling Ground Pt.2 (some versions/pressings list him). (Discogs)
Mixing — public credits rarely show a separate mix engineer. Interviews/AMA material and release notes emphasise that Sabre & Stray handle production and the creative/sonic decisions themselves, which suggests they either mix in-house or use uncredited/label mastering houses for final polish. (Reddit)
Notes & caveats
Discogs often lists credits by specific release/pressing. That means different pressings or label releases can have different mastering credits (e.g., lacquer cut / vinyl mastering might credit a different person than the digital master). Always check the exact release page (vinyl vs digital vs reissue). (Discogs)
Bandcamp / official release pages sometimes omit detailed engineering credits — for full liner-note detail, Discogs entries and physical release inserts are usually the best source. (Ivy Lab)

?? 2. How they reach that depth of clarity
Their mix quality comes from engineering fundamentals applied obsessively. Here’s what they’re doing differently:
A. Sound selection before mixing
They choose or design sounds that already fit — they “mix at the source.”
Heavy use of mid-range focused drums, textural basses, and minimal clutter.
If you solo their stems, each sound occupies its own frequency zone — there’s almost no fighting for space.
B. Surgical frequency control
They use multi-band transient shaping, dynamic EQ, and parallel saturation to keep warmth without masking.
Their “clean dirt” sound is a product of distortion ? EQ cleanup ? multiband compression chains.
C. Spatial precision
Subtle mid/side EQ and micro-panning.
Slightly asymmetrical stereo placement — hi-hats 2–5% off-center, atmospheres mid/side widened but phase-safe.
Occasional reverb modulation or “pre-delayed” reverbs that keep clarity while adding space.
D. Reference culture
They famously reference their own mixes against:
Alix Perez, Noisia, Benga, Burial
And even non-D&B mixes (hip-hop, trap, R&B) for low-end and vocal feel.
E. Controlled bus processing
### Their mix bus is probably light
Transparent limiter (e.g., FabFilter Pro-L2 or Ozone Maximizer)
1–2 dB gentle compression, then clipping on export.
Most of the glue happens in group buses, not the master.

?? 3. How you can develop that depth
Here’s the Ivy Lab-inspired path that actually works:
Limit yourself to 15–20 samples and make them sound huge.
You’ll learn layering, EQ, and spacing faster than from tutorials.
Use reference tracks — import “Teacup,” “Cake,” or “Magikess” and compare loudness, balance, and width.
Build a “mixing template” that mimics theirs:
Drum bus with light glue comp (e.g., SSL-style)
Parallel distortion return
Sub group with mono utility
FX bus with filtered reverb + tempo delay
A/B constantly at low volume — Ivy Lab mixes sound wide even quietly; train for that.
Study transient control — transient shapers, soft clipping, OTT (used sparingly).
Analyze with a goniometer and spectrum analyzer (SPAN, Youlean, etc.) for stereo/low-end consistency.
