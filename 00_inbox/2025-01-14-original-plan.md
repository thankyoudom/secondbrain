# Original Plan

That’s great you’re setting a goal for a marathon in 2026! With plenty of time to build up, we can focus on gradually increasing your mileage and endurance while avoiding injury. Given that you’re currently running 3 miles as your max, we’ll start with a plan to safely increase your running distance over the next few years.

Below is a **long-term marathon training plan** spread out over a few phases:

### **Phase 1: Base Building (6–12 months)**
This phase is about building a strong running foundation. We’ll focus on easy-paced runs, building endurance, and strengthening muscles to prevent injury.

### **Weekly Structure (4–5 days of running)**
- **Monday:** Rest or cross-training (cycling, swimming, yoga)
- **Tuesday:** Easy run (start with 2–3 miles, gradually increase to 5 miles over 12 weeks)
- **Wednesday:** Cross-training (bike, swim, or strength training)
- **Thursday:** Easy run (2–3 miles)
- **Friday:** Rest
- **Saturday:** Long run (start with 3 miles, increase by 1 mile every 2–3 weeks)
- **Sunday:** Active recovery (walk, light jog, or rest)

**Key Focus:**
- Build mileage slowly (don’t increase by more than 10% per week).
- Keep a relaxed, conversational pace during most runs.
- Add some core strength and leg strength exercises (lunges, squats, planks).

### **Phase 2: Increasing Distance & Speed (12–18 months)**
At this stage, you’ll be able to run longer distances. The focus now will be on gradually increasing your long run and introducing some speed work.

### **Weekly Structure (5–6 days of running)**
- **Monday:** Rest
- **Tuesday:** Tempo run (start with 3–4 miles at a pace 30–45 seconds faster than your normal pace).
- **Wednesday:** Easy run (4–5 miles).
- **Thursday:** Interval training (1-mile warm-up, then 4–6 x 400m intervals with rest in between).
- **Friday:** Rest
- **Saturday:** Long run (increase to 6–10 miles, add 1–2 miles every 2–3 weeks).
- **Sunday:** Recovery run (easy pace, 3–5 miles).

**Key Focus:**
- Work on running at a faster pace during tempo and interval sessions.
- Increase your long run distance gradually.
- Pay attention to recovery, nutrition, and hydration as the intensity picks up.

### **Phase 3: Marathon-Specific Training (18–24 months)**
By now, you’ve built a solid base, and you’re ready to focus more on marathon-specific training.

### **Weekly Structure (5–6 days of running)**
- **Monday:** Rest
- **Tuesday:** Interval training or hill sprints (6–8 x 400m intervals or 8–10 hill sprints).
- **Wednesday:** Mid-distance run (5–8 miles).
- **Thursday:** Tempo run (5–8 miles).
- **Friday:** Rest or cross-training.
- **Saturday:** Long run (gradually increasing to 15–20 miles over the next 12 months).
- **Sunday:** Recovery run (4–6 miles).

**Key Focus:**
- Incorporate marathon-specific fueling strategies (gels, hydration).
- Practice running at marathon pace for parts of your long run.
- Focus on pacing, strategy, and mental preparation.

### **Phase 4: Tapering & Peak Training (6–8 months before race)**
As you get closer to your race in October 2026, you’ll transition to a more specific, race-focused training plan.

### **Weekly Structure (5–6 days of running)**
- **Monday:** Rest
- **Tuesday:** Interval or tempo run (work on pace for marathon goals).
- **Wednesday:** Mid-long run (8–12 miles).
- **Thursday:** Easy run or cross-training.
- **Friday:** Rest
- **Saturday:** Long run (peak at 20-22 miles 3–4 weeks before the marathon).
- **Sunday:** Recovery run.

**Key Focus:**
- Tapering (gradually reduce mileage in the final 3–4 weeks before race day).
- Mental training and race-day strategy.

### **Additional Tips:**
1. **Strength Training:** Incorporate 2-3 days of strength training per week to prevent injury, especially focusing on legs, core, and glutes.
2. **Mobility Work:** Stretching, yoga, and foam rolling will help maintain flexibility and recovery.
3. **Listen to Your Body:** If you’re feeling any discomfort or pain, adjust your plan to allow for proper recovery.
4. **Fueling:** As your mileage increases, begin to practice your fueling during long runs (energy gels, hydration, etc.) to mimic race conditions.

You’ll have plenty of time to adapt to increasing mileage and to test what works best for you. As you progress, we can adjust your plan further to ensure you’re well-prepared for race day!

Let me know if you’d like to get more specific with any phase or need help adjusting it as you go!
