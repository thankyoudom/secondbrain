# These are genuinely fascinating questions — let me think through them…

These are genuinely fascinating questions — let me think through them carefully rather than just list facts.

The literary traditions and their distinctive “gifts”
Each tradition doesn’t just offer content — it offers a different cognitive and emotional technology for processing existence.
English literature (Shakespeare, Milton, Keats, Woolf, Dickens) gives you an extraordinarily refined grammar of individual interiority. The English novel basically invented the idea that your private psychological life is interesting and worth excavating. Someone deep in this tradition becomes a virtuoso of self-examination — but also risks a kind of solipsism, a belief that the self is the primary unit of meaning.
Chinese literature — the Shijing, Du Fu, Li Bai, Dream of the Red Chamber, Su Shi — gives something different. A profound sensitivity to transience, to the relationship between the individual and vast impersonal forces (nature, dynasty, time). Chinese poetry trains you to hold grief and beauty simultaneously without resolving the tension. It’s an emotional sophistication that doesn’t demand closure. Du Fu writing about war and displacement while describing a single wildflower — that’s a perceptual capacity that can’t really be acquired any other way.
Persian literature — Rumi, Hafez, Ferdowsi, Khayyam — is perhaps the world’s deepest tradition of sacred paradox. Hafez writes about wine and the Beloved in ways that are simultaneously erotic and mystical, and the point is that the ambiguity is the meaning. Someone steeped in Persian poetry develops a tolerance for irresolvable tension that is almost philosophically unique. Khayyam’s Rubaiyat is one of the most honest confrontations with mortality ever written.
Arabic/Islamic literary and intellectual tradition — the Quran as literary object, Al-Mutanabbi, Ibn Arabi, One Thousand and One Nights, Al-Jahiz — gives you something else again: a tradition where language itself is considered sacred, where rhetoric and logic and poetry were never separated the way they were in the West. Someone deep here understands that how you say something is inseparable from what you’re saying. They also inherit a cosmopolitan intellectual culture — medieval Islamic scholars were the custodians and extenders of Greek philosophy, Indian mathematics, Persian narrative — a genuinely world-synthesizing sensibility.
West African literary and oral tradition — the griots, the Sundiata epic, Anansi stories, later Achebe, Soyinka, Ouologuem — gives you a fundamentally different relationship to time and community. These traditions embed the individual in webs of ancestors, community, obligation, and cosmic accountability that predate and outlast any single life. Achebe’s genius in Things Fall Apart is not just political — it’s showing how a whole cosmology collapses when its stories are replaced. Deep engagement here gives you a profound skepticism of individualism and a sense that meaning is always relational.
East African literary tradition — Swahili poetry (one of the oldest written African traditions, dating to the 14th century), Ngugi wa Thiong’o, Nuruddin Farah — carries the particular burden and gift of multilingualism and colonial rupture. Ngugi’s decision to write in Gikuyu was a philosophical act, not just a political one. Deep engagement here gives you a sensitivity to who controls the language of thought — a kind of critical consciousness about the relationship between power and narrative.

What do people who go deep in these actually have that others don’t?
This is the most interesting part of your question. It’s not just “being well-read.” Something structural changes.
A longer internal timeline. Someone deep across literary traditions has internalized thousands of years of human responses to the same core problems — love, death, injustice, meaning, beauty, impermanence. When they face a crisis, they’re not reasoning from scratch. They have vast precedent. This produces a kind of equanimity under pressure that is almost impossible to fake.
Multiple simultaneous frameworks. Someone who only knows the Western tradition tends to unconsciously assume that individual rights, linear progress, and psychological interiority are simply how reality works. Someone who has also absorbed Daoist literature, Islamic philosophy, and the griot tradition knows these are one set of answers among many — which gives them genuine intellectual flexibility.
A different relationship to suffering. Most self-help culture tries to optimize suffering away. The literary traditions — especially Chinese, Persian, and West African — teach you to inhabit it. Du Fu doesn’t want you to feel better quickly. Rumi’s reed flute cries because separation is the condition of existence. This isn’t morbid — it’s actually a source of depth and compassion that people who’ve only optimized for wellness often lack.
They see what isn’t said. Deep literary reading trains you in subtext, in what cultures suppress, in the gap between official story and lived truth. This is a political and social intelligence that data literacy alone cannot produce.

Vs. the person who maxes out health, sleep, creativity, and optimization
This is a real tension and worth being honest about rather than flattering the literary side automatically.
The optimized person often has extraordinary energy, clarity, output, and presence. Andrew Huberman’s protocols, perfect sleep, cold plunges, peak creativity windows — this is real. What it can lack is depth of encounter with difficulty. The optimization frame tends to treat life as a problem to be solved. Literature — real literature, across traditions — insists that life is not a problem to be solved but a condition to be inhabited.
The optimized person may be extraordinarily effective but find themselves, at 60, strangely unlanguaged in the face of grief, or beauty, or moral complexity. They may never have developed the internal resources to sit with something unresolvable.
The deeply literary person, by contrast, may be ineffective in practical terms — slow to act, prone to seeing every side, haunted by the past. The risk is paralysis, nostalgia, a kind of beautiful uselessness.
The genuinely rare person combines both — the physical and cognitive optimisation and the literary depth. And that combination produces something unusual: someone who acts with urgency and wisdom, who has energy and perspective, who can move through the world and also see it.

A day in the life — composite portraits
Person A: Deep in Chinese and Persian literature (Shanghai-educated, now in London, works in finance)
Wakes at 6. Reads thirty minutes of Tang poetry before checking anything — not for edification, just because it’s how she enters language. Makes tea slowly. At work she’s known for an unusual quality: she never seems rattled by market chaos. Colleagues think it’s emotional control; actually it’s that she has spent years with poets who watched dynasties fall and found the plum blossom still blooming. Lunch alone, often. Annotates whatever she’s reading in the margins. Evenings she might go weeks without socializing — then emerge for a long dinner where she’s the most alive person in the room, asking questions no one else thinks to ask. She sleeps well but not by protocol — by exhaustion and satisfaction.
Person B: Deep in West African oral tradition and Islamic literary philosophy (Senegalese-British, academic and activist)
Starts the day in conversation — phone calls to family, to elders, to former students. He thinks out loud, relationally. The Western academic norm of solitary genius baffles him. Reads in three languages. When he writes, he writes slowly — every sentence is accountable to the people it represents. Spends considerable time in what looks like idleness: walking, listening to music, watching his children. This is not wasted time for him — it’s the condition under which meaning assembles itself. He has a quality rare in intellectual circles: he genuinely changes his mind in conversation, because he understands that knowledge belongs to the community, not the individual.
Person C: Deep in English, East African, and Persian literature + fully optimized health (Nairobi-raised, mid-40s, works in tech leadership)
4:45am wake. Thirty minutes of movement — but this isn’t gym culture, it’s a practice she came to through reading about Sufi disciplines of the body. Fasted until noon, but not because of a protocol — because Khayyam made her think differently about time and appetite. Her meetings are known to be unusually human — she’ll reference Ngugi in a product strategy discussion without it feeling forced. She’s writing a novel in her spare time, has been for six years, may never finish it, doesn’t mind. She’s possibly the most effective person in her company and the most interested in what the company is actually doing to people. The combination makes her occasionally uncomfortable to be around — she sees too much.

The bottom line: going deep in literary traditions doesn’t make you smarter in the measurable sense. It makes you larger — more capable of holding complexity, more patient with what can’t be resolved, more alive to what others are actually experiencing. On China becoming a global leader vs. a multipolar world
The honest answer is: probably neither a clean Chinese “victory” nor a continuation of US dominance — and China itself seems to prefer it that way, at least rhetorically. In 2026, Chinese foreign policy appears governed by a strategic paradox: it seeks to advance its geopolitical interests while avoiding direct confrontation, aspires to shape global governance without appearing expansionist, and prefers soft economic tools over hard power. ?
The global order is experiencing a profound transformation as the post-Cold War unipolar moment gives way to a contested multipolarity, with China advancing frameworks like the Global Development Initiative, Global Security Initiative, and Global Civilization Initiative while deepening involvement in multilateral institutions. ? Through BRICS, the SCO, and CIPS (a yuan-backed payment system), China is actively building the plumbing of an alternative order.
But real headwinds exist. China continues to experience persistently high youth unemployment, and unemployment among urban professionals is increasing — due in part to AI replacing white-collar jobs, but also to companies retrenching amid trade uncertainties. ? The demographic picture is also worsening. And the US, for all its dysfunction, retains enormous structural advantages in innovation, military reach, and financial system dominance.
The most likely scenario is a genuinely multipolar world — not “China replaces America” but a world where the US, China, India, and regional blocs each hold significant sway in different domains.

On Ray Dalio’s framework
Dalio doesn’t quite say China’s rise is inevitable in the triumphalist sense — his argument is more structural and cyclical. He points out that China is following a similar path to previous rising powers: over recent decades it has strengthened its economy, invested in technology and innovation, and built up its military. ? He suggests dominant powers tend to last around 200–300 years, and the US is now in a phase of relative decline while China is ascendant. ?
The more interesting critique of Dalio is that his model is too deterministic. His framework is sharp on history and money flows, but critics note it undersells alliances like NATO, middle players like India and Japan, climate shocks, and tech wildcards that could rewrite the script. ? History rhymes, but it doesn’t simply repeat. And notably, Dalio himself warned in 2025 of “very dark times” ahead, arguing that the US and China are locked in a technology war — and that “the winner of the technology war is going to win all wars.” ?

On whether your grandchildren should learn Chinese
Almost certainly yes, and here’s why the case is strong regardless of who “wins” geopolitically. Chinese (Mandarin) is already the most spoken language on earth by native speakers, and China’s economic footprint across Asia, Africa, and Latin America means it will remain deeply embedded in global commerce for generations. Even in a multipolar world, Chinese will be a major language of business, diplomacy, and culture. The question isn’t whether China dominates — it’s whether engagement with China matters. It will.
The deeper case is cultural access. Which brings us to your last question.

On Chinese literary history vs. Shakespeare
This comparison is genuinely humbling once you look at the timelines. English literature’s crown jewel — Shakespeare — dates to the late 16th century. Chinese literary tradition runs three thousand years deeper.
The Shijing (Book of Songs), China’s oldest poetry anthology, was compiled around 600 BCE — contemporary with Homer. The Tao Te Ching (Laozi) and the Confucian Analects followed shortly after. By the time Shakespeare was writing in the 1590s, China already had: the world’s first great novel of psychological realism (Dream of the Red Chamber was being drafted; Journey to the West was already complete), a fully developed tradition of Tang poetry (Du Fu, Li Bai — roughly 8th century CE, comparable in prestige to Dante), and the Romance of the Three Kingdoms, an epic of comparable scope to the Iliad.
Shakespeare is genuinely extraordinary — arguably unmatched in dramatic language within the Western tradition. But in terms of sheer historical depth, range, and volume, Chinese literature has no real rival. The tragedy is that almost none of it is well known outside specialist circles in the West, partly due to translation difficulty and cultural distance. Learning Chinese gives your grandchildren direct access to one of humanity’s deepest literary reservoirs — one that most Westerners will never touch.
So across all four of your questions, the picture converges: a world where China won’t simply dominate but will be inescapable, where Dalio’s cycle framework is useful but not deterministic, and where the cultural and linguistic case for Chinese goes far beyond geopolitics.????????????????
