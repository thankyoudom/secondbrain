# It is a frustrating feeling when your brain feels like it’s on dial…

It is a frustrating feeling when your brain feels like it’s on dial-up while everyone else at the table is on fiber—especially when you know you have the "hardware" to keep up.
That "one speed slower" feeling usually isn't about intelligence; it’s about processing speed and word retrieval. Think of it like a muscle that needs to be "warmed up" so the path from thought to speech becomes a highway instead of a dirt road.
Here are a few high-impact habits to sharpen your conversational "ping" this year.
1. The "Shadowing" Technique
Reading out loud is good, but shadowing is better. Listen to a podcast or a fast-paced speaker (like a comedian or a sharp interviewer) and try to repeat what they say exactly as they say it, with only a half-second delay.
- Why it works: It forces your brain to process language and motor movements simultaneously at a high tempo.
- Action: Do this for 5 minutes during your morning commute or while getting ready.
2. The "Video-a-Day" (with a Twist)
Jack Dorsey’s habit is great, but the magic is in the review. If you record a video of yourself retelling a story from your day:
- Watch for "Processing Gaps": Note where you say "um," "uh," or just trail off.
- The 2-Minute Drill: Pick a random object (e.g., a stapler) and force yourself to talk about it for 60 seconds without stopping. It trains your brain to find words under pressure.
3. Narrative Journaling
Standard journaling ("Today I went to the store") helps with mental health, but Narrative Journaling helps with communication.
- The Goal: Write down one specific interaction from your day as if you were telling it as a story at a dinner party.
- Why it works: It pre-loads your "anecdote library" so you aren’t searching for the right way to phrase things in real-time.
4. Expansion & Contraction Games
Communication speed often stalls because we get stuck trying to find the perfect word.
- The Game: Take a simple sentence (e.g., "The food was good") and try to say it five different ways in ten seconds.
- Example: "That meal was top-tier," "I really enjoyed the flavors," "The chef nailed the seasoning," etc.
5. Strategic Listening (The "Next Step" Habit)
Often, we feel slow because we are so busy thinking about what to say that we stop listening.
- The Habit: While someone is talking, challenge yourself to identify the emotion behind their words rather than the facts.
- Result: You’ll find that your responses become more intuitive and less "calculated," which naturally speeds up your delivery.
Summary of Your Daily Stack
| Habit | Time Investment | Focus Area |
|---|---|---|
| Shadowing | 5 Mins | Verbal Fluidity & Tempo |
| Reading Out Loud | 10 Mins | Pronunciation & Confidence |
| Video/Audio Log | 3 Mins | Self-Awareness & Fillers |
| Narrative Journaling | 10 Mins | Storytelling & Retrieval |
One quick thought on the music: Recording music is a fantastic idea. It forces you to think about rhythm and cadence. If you find yourself "dragging" in conversations, practicing a verse with a strict tempo can actually sub-consciously train your brain to keep pace with the "beat" of a conversation.
