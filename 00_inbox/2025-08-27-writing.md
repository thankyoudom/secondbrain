1. Skull and Bones — Do they “mostly just write essays”? What’s their curriculum like?
Officially, Skull and Bones is a secretive senior society at Yale University, with no public curriculum or formal assignments like essay writing (Wikipedia). However, campus folklore and student discussions hint at a different story:
### On GreekRank, a student-commenter claims
“Skull and Bones writes essays because obviously… they have these pretentious traditions. … Desmos is pretty serious and they have every write essays in response to the weekly bios.”
### Another adds
“They… focused on extremely long bios, essay writing, group discussions, etc.” (Greekrank)
### That said, others caution
“Not just Desmos literally every society… ask you to write a response to the person who just gave a bio — could be a paragraph or a letter.” (Greekrank)
Bottom line: There's no formal, confirmed "curriculum" for Skull and Bones. But anecdotal reports suggest that many members—within Skull and Bones and similar Yale societies—engage in reflective writing or graduating class bios (essentially extended personal statements), group discussions, and possibly weekly written responses. This isn’t official, but it’s part of how members process and share personal narratives.

2. Equivalent groups and their practices
At Yale, the so-called "Big Three" senior societies are:
Skull and Bones
Scroll and Key
Wolf’s Head
These groups are steeped in tradition and select members in their junior year. Their internal practices are closely guarded, but campus lore suggests similarly intense social and intellectual rituals—like speeches, long bios, or writing—but there's no public "curriculum" available (Wikipedia).
Other traditional or debating societies (sometimes referred to as “Desmos” in student slang) apparently require essay-like autobiographies or thoughtful written responses. Discussions and eloquent narratives seem to be a core feature across multiple societies—even if it’s informal (Greekrank).

3. Want to write more—build a strong daily or weekly habit
Inspired by these traditions, here's a lightweight routine you can try:
Daily micro-writing (10–20 min): Reflect on your day, jot thoughts, or write brief responses to prompts like: "What challenged me today?" or "One idea I want to explore further."
Weekly deeper piece (30–60 min): Similar to the “bio” tradition—craft a well-thought-out essay or personal narrative (it could be about a topic you care about, a personal reflection, or a creative piece).
Sharing or reflecting: Even if not in a secret society, sharing your work with a friend or journal can create accountability and added meaning.
By weaving shorter daily writing with a weekly richer piece, you’ll build consistency and depth—just like how traditions in these societies seem to encourage reflection over time.
