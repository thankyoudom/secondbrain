# It’s easy to get cynical and see every image as a "brand asset," but…

It’s easy to get cynical and see every image as a "brand asset," but illustration has historically been the heavy lifter for human progress. Before we had high-speed cameras or even widespread literacy, illustration was how we mapped the soul, the stars, and the internal organs.
Here are a few examples of illustrations that didn’t just look good—they changed how we function as a species.
1. Florence Nightingale’s "Coxcomb" Diagram (1858)
While we know her as the "Lady with the Lamp," Nightingale was actually a pioneer of data visualization.
- The Impact: During the Crimean War, she created a polar area diagram (the "Coxcomb") to show that most soldiers weren't dying from battle wounds, but from preventable diseases caused by poor sanitation.
- The Result: She used this illustration to "lobby" the British government. It was so easy to understand that it led to a total overhaul of military hospital hygiene, saving countless lives. It turned dry statistics into a visual call to action.
2. Andreas Vesalius’s De humani corporis fabrica (1543)
Before Vesalius, medical knowledge was largely based on ancient texts and the dissection of animals.
- The Impact: Vesalius collaborated with artists (likely from Titian’s studio) to create incredibly detailed, "flayed" illustrations of the human body in active, lifelike poses.
- The Result: This wasn't just art; it was the first accurate "user manual" for the human body. It corrected centuries of medical errors and laid the foundation for modern surgery and anatomy.
3. Harry Beck’s London Underground Map (1933)
If you’ve ever looked at a subway map in NYC, Tokyo, or Paris, you’re looking at Harry Beck’s homework.
- The Impact: Early transit maps were geographical nightmares—they tried to show exactly where the tunnels were underground, which resulted in a cluttered mess. Beck, an engineering draftsman, realized that passengers didn't care where they were geographically; they just needed to know how to get from point A to point B.
- The Result: He simplified the map into a clean, "circuit board" style diagram with 45-degree angles. It revolutionized information design by prioritizing the user's mental model over physical reality.
4. The Pioneer Plaque (1972)
This is perhaps the most "ambitious" illustration ever created.
- The Impact: Designed by Carl Sagan and Frank Drake (and illustrated by Linda Salzman Sagan), this gold-anodized aluminum plaque was placed on the Pioneer 10 and 11 spacecraft.
- The Result: It’s a visual message intended for extraterrestrial life, using line drawings to explain our location in the galaxy and what we look like. It serves as a "Message in a Bottle" for the entire human race, showing that illustration is our universal language.
5. Thomas Nast’s "Tammany Tiger" (1871)
Illustration can also be a weapon for justice.
- The Impact: "Boss" Tweed was a corrupt politician who ran New York City's Tammany Hall. He famously didn't care what newspapers wrote about him because his constituents couldn't read, but he said, "My constituents can’t help seeing them damn pictures!"
- The Result: Nast’s brutal caricatures of Tweed's corruption were so effective that they turned public opinion against him, eventually leading to his arrest. It proved that a single illustration could topple a corrupt empire.
