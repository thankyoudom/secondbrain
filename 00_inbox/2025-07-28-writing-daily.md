# writing daily

Daily rapping, recording, or songwriting might seem like just a creative outlet, but the practice has a host of underlooked benefits that go far beyond simply making music. Here's a breakdown of the mental, emotional, and philosophical advantages over the span of a year, and how they can positively impact your confidence, mindset, and overall growth:
1. Mental Benefits
Improved Focus and Discipline: Setting a daily goal to write or record something keeps your brain sharp. It's a form of mental exercise that requires consistency, which strengthens your ability to focus on tasks over long periods. Daily practice helps develop discipline, and that can carry over to other areas of life.
Stress Relief & Emotional Processing: Writing lyrics or rapping can act as a form of self-therapy. It’s a safe space for venting frustrations, processing emotions, and making sense of life experiences. Over time, this practice can reduce stress levels and provide a healthy outlet for pent-up feelings.
Cognitive Flexibility & Creativity: Every day you push your mind to think differently, find new metaphors, and craft fresh rhymes. This trains your brain to be more adaptable and creative, which can have spill-over effects in problem-solving and decision-making in everyday life.
2. Confidence Boost
Public Speaking & Self-Expression: Rapping or performing your own lyrics in private or to an audience is a powerful way to improve your communication skills. Over time, your confidence in public speaking and self-expression grows exponentially. The act of delivering lyrics, whether in front of a mirror or recording a track, reinforces the ability to share your thoughts openly.
Overcoming Fear of Judgment: Starting off, you might feel nervous or self-conscious about your skills or how others will perceive your music. But doing this every day helps you become less worried about judgment and more focused on your personal growth. The act of recording is a constant reminder that perfection doesn’t come overnight and it’s okay to make mistakes along the way.
Skill Mastery = Confidence: As you look back at your progress over the course of a year, you will be able to see the dramatic improvement in your flow, lyricism, and sound. Watching your growth gives you the confidence that anything you put your mind to can be developed and refined.
3. Year-Long Differences
Trackable Growth: At the start, you might struggle with writer's block, weak punchlines, or a lack of flow. But after a year of consistent practice, you’ll look back and notice that your cadence is smoother, your metaphors are more intricate, and your rhyming is sharper. You’ll be amazed at how much you’ve evolved, both as an artist and as a person.
Mastering the Craft: Creativity is a muscle that gets stronger the more you work it. By committing to daily writing or rapping, you build an understanding of rhythm, rhyme schemes, and song structure. Your songwriting will likely become more sophisticated over time as you experiment with different styles and approaches.
Personal Growth in the Process: Over the year, your perspective on life might shift. The lyrics you wrote when you first started may have been more about immediate frustrations, but after a year, you might be reflecting on deeper philosophical or universal topics. The process of writing daily encourages a kind of introspection that helps you better understand your personal growth.
4. Daily Goal-Setting
Micro Wins: Setting small daily goals (e.g., writing 16 bars, recording one verse, or brainstorming ideas for a hook) creates momentum. Even if the day’s output isn’t perfect, you’ll still feel a sense of accomplishment because you achieved your goal. These small wins accumulate and contribute to a greater sense of productivity and satisfaction.
Building a Routine: Making songwriting or rapping a daily habit instills a sense of routine, which is essential for building any skill. It turns creativity into something you show up for, even on days when you feel less inspired. Creating a productive routine around music can also help improve other areas of your life by instilling similar habits.
5. Philosophy Behind Daily Practice
Embracing Imperfection: The idea of "perfect" lyrics or raps is a fallacy. Through daily practice, you learn to embrace imperfection. The goal isn’t always to create something flawless, but to keep creating, keep learning, and keep evolving.
The Concept of "The Process": One of the philosophical lessons songwriting teaches is the idea of focusing on the process rather than the end result. It’s about how you evolve through the act of creating, not just waiting for success to materialize. This lesson can be applied to any aspect of life—personal, professional, or artistic. It’s about being present in the moment and trusting that the results will come with time.
Flow State: Rapping or writing can help you enter a flow state—where time seems to disappear, and you’re completely immersed in the task at hand. Getting into this mindset daily can lead to breakthroughs not only in creativity but also in how you approach other tasks in your life.
6. Additional Long-Term Benefits
Networking & Collaboration: If you decide to share your work online or perform in local venues, daily practice can help you hone a unique style that makes you stand out. Over time, this might open the door to collaborations, gigs, or a following, further boosting your confidence and creative output.
Mental Health Improvement: Having a consistent, creative outlet provides a sense of purpose and achievement, which is linked to improved mental health. It becomes a constructive way to process the world around you, helping you deal with anxiety, depression, or other challenges.
Self-Discovery & Authenticity: Through the lens of songwriting, you can better understand who you are and what you stand for. What you choose to write about is a reflection of your thoughts, emotions, and values. Over a year, your lyrics will likely evolve, and this transformation mirrors your own journey towards authenticity.

Suggested Approach for the First Year
Start Small: Don’t aim for perfection immediately. Set daily goals (e.g., write a verse or freestyle for 10 minutes).
Track Progress: Keep a notebook or recording log to document your thoughts, feelings, and improvements. After a few months, review your work and take note of your growth.
Experiment: Try different styles, beats, and lyrical themes. The point is to push boundaries and discover what works for you.
Reflect: At the end of the year, look back at the first song you wrote and compare it with your most recent work. Recognizing the progress you’ve made will keep you motivated for the next year.
In short, daily rapping, recording, and songwriting offer much more than artistic expression—they shape your mindset, improve your discipline, and build your confidence in ways that extend well beyond music. The benefits accumulate quietly, but by the end of a year, the transformation is tangible, and the habits you’ve cultivated will likely benefit many other aspects of your life.______

Personal Growth in Songwriting/Rapping: 1 Year vs 3 Years
Year 1: Learning the Basics
Lyricism & Flow: In the first year, you're still figuring out your voice. Your flow might be a bit rough around the edges, and the lyrics could be surface-level or repetitive as you're learning how to write within the confines of a beat and rhyme scheme. You're getting the hang of structure, such as verses, choruses, hooks, and bridges.
Mindset: There’s a lot of excitement and experimentation. It’s a time of trying new things and learning what works and what doesn’t. Confidence isn’t fully there yet, and there's an internal struggle to "find your style."
Technical Skills: Rhyming is a priority. You’re more likely to focus on wordplay, punchlines, and trying to keep up with the rhythm, which might result in overthinking things.
Year 3: Refining & Deepening the Craft
Lyricism & Flow: By this time, your flow has improved significantly. You have a much better understanding of how to manipulate rhythms and structure your lines. Your lyrics become more personal, introspective, and maybe even philosophical. You begin to develop a unique voice that stands out.
Mindset: Your confidence has grown. You’ve probably faced some self-doubt and have learned how to turn that into motivation. There’s a more purposeful approach to writing; you’re no longer just trying to be "good," but aiming for something deeper and more impactful.
Technical Skills: You’ve got a better handle on using metaphors, similes, and storytelling. The delivery has matured, and you might start experimenting with different cadences and flows that fit your evolving sound.
Mental Improvements
Cognitive Flexibility: Over time, rapping/songwriting will help you develop a higher level of cognitive flexibility. This means that you’re more comfortable with adapting, thinking on your feet, and improvising both in the creative process and when performing.
Self-Reflection: Writing regularly forces you to look inward. You begin to question your thoughts, experiences, and how they fit into larger narratives. This leads to better self-awareness and emotional intelligence. It’s almost like having daily therapy through your lyrics.
Mental Resilience: Getting better at rapping or songwriting often involves handling rejection and self-doubt. Over time, this builds mental toughness, teaching you not to take things personally and to learn from failures.
Daily Habits
Writing 16 Bars: This is a standard practice, but you’ll want to mix it up to avoid burnout. Writing regularly—whether it’s 16 bars, a full song, or just a verse—builds consistency.
Freewriting/Stream of Consciousness: This is one of the best ways to tap into raw creativity. You can use this as a daily exercise to explore new ideas or to break through creative blocks.
Mindfulness: Incorporating mindfulness can actually improve your writing. Meditation or breathwork can clear mental clutter and make space for more nuanced ideas. The clarity you develop here can enhance your lyricism and make it more profound.
Reading Philosophy: You mentioned philosopher poems. Reading philosophers like Nietzsche, Camus, or even more contemporary thinkers like Alain de Botton could provide a treasure trove of intellectual material to build into your lyrics. The daily reading habit also sharpens your cognitive processes and brings fresh perspectives to your writing.
Journaling: Reflect on your day, your emotions, or philosophical questions. This kind of journaling could fuel your lyrics with more authenticity and depth.
Philosophy Behind It All
Authenticity vs. Marketability: As you progress, you'll face the balancing act between staying true to yourself and your message versus the pressures of the industry or expectations of fans. Philosophically, this aligns with the concept of the authentic self in existentialism. Are you writing to express yourself, or are you writing to fit in? The more you develop, the clearer your answer to this question will be.
Art as Reflection: As you dive deeper into philosophy, you might encounter ideas like Plato’s notion of art being a form of imitation. As a rapper or songwriter, you might begin seeing your lyrics as a reflection of the human condition. Or you could draw inspiration from something like Jung’s ideas on the collective unconscious, where your writing taps into universal human themes.
The Transience of Creativity: Philosophers like Heraclitus, who stated that "everything flows," could influence your outlook on the process. The creative process is constantly evolving, and a huge part of growth comes from learning to embrace this impermanence.
Creative Integrity: The practice of writing every day might reflect something akin to Kant’s idea of a moral duty or responsibility—being true to your craft, regardless of outside validation. This can be a source of mental resilience and fulfillment.
