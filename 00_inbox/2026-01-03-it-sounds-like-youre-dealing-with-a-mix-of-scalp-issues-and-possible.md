# It sounds like you’re dealing with a mix of scalp issues and possible…

It sounds like you’re dealing with a mix of scalp issues and possible candida or yeast-related problems. I can definitely help break this down for you.
Aloe for Candida and Scalp Issues
Aloe vera is known for its soothing properties, and it can help calm irritated skin, whether it's on your scalp or elsewhere. However, aloe is generally more of a topical soothing agent, and while it has some antifungal properties, it’s not the main go-to treatment for candida over time. It’s more likely to give relief for mild skin irritation or inflammation rather than solving an internal fungal infection like candida. So, if you’re using it on your scalp, it might help reduce irritation but won’t necessarily "cure" a fungal issue.
For internal candida, people often use things like antifungal supplements or changes in diet (cutting out sugar, for example). But using aloe as a topical treatment for your scalp could still be a good thing to try in the short term for its soothing effects. You might also want to check out other antifungal shampoos, especially those with ingredients like ketoconazole or tea tree oil.
Scalp Issues and "Blood Things"
As for your barber saying it’s a "blood thing," there could be some truth to that. While blood itself might not be the main cause, scalp problems (like dandruff, seborrheic dermatitis, or fungal infections) can sometimes be linked to underlying health issues. Poor circulation, certain hormones, and even diet can affect your scalp and hair health. So, while it's not exactly a "blood thing" in a literal sense, factors like nutrition, hormonal imbalances, or blood circulation can affect how your skin reacts.
If it’s a recurring problem, getting a checkup with a dermatologist or doctor might be a good idea to see if there’s an underlying issue contributing to the scalp problem.
Hibiscus Tea & Cloves
Hibiscus tea with cloves is an interesting combo! Hibiscus has antioxidants and may help with blood circulation and detoxification, while cloves have antifungal and antibacterial properties. Drinking it might help with overall immune system support, which can be beneficial when dealing with something like candida. It could also support your scalp health, but it’s not a direct antifungal treatment.
For other natural antifungal suggestions, you could try:
Garlic: Known for its antifungal properties. You could incorporate it into your diet.
Apple cider vinegar (diluted): Some people use it as a scalp rinse to reduce fungal growth and balance pH.
Coconut oil: Has antifungal properties, and you could apply it topically to your scalp.
Also, if you're dealing with a lot of yeast-related issues, a good diet change might help as well—avoiding sugars, refined carbs, and processed foods can reduce candida growth.
Other Recommendations
Tea tree oil: You can try using a small amount in your shampoo or as a topical treatment for your scalp. It's a potent antifungal.
Probiotics: If you're dealing with candida, a good probiotic can help balance your gut flora, which in turn helps control yeast overgrowth.
Hydration: Sometimes issues with skin and hair can be linked to dehydration, so drinking enough water is key.
If things keep getting worse or don't improve, it's best to consult with a healthcare professional to rule out anything more serious.
