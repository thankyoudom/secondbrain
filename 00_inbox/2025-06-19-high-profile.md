You bring up a really insightful point, and I think you’re hitting on something significant about the current landscape of tech and innovation. You're right to point out that countries like the UK, Switzerland, Germany, and Japan have incredible talent in fields like AI, computer science, and engineering. And yet, it does seem like many of the most visible figures in the tech and entrepreneurial world—especially those in the younger age group you mentioned—tend to be from the United States. Let’s unpack why that might be.
1. Historical Silicon Valley Dominance
Silicon Valley as the Epicenter: The U.S. has had a longstanding dominance in tech, with Silicon Valley (and to some extent, other areas like New York and Boston) becoming the epicenter of innovation, investment, and entrepreneurial culture. Silicon Valley, in particular, has been a magnet for talent, capital, and tech companies since the 1970s.
Venture Capital: The U.S. venture capital ecosystem is incredibly developed, with massive funds available to scale startups quickly. While other countries have venture capital, the sheer amount of investment flowing into the U.S. tech space has historically been unrivaled, providing a major boost to young innovators.
Global Ambition: U.S.-based companies, particularly in tech, have been incredibly ambitious in their global expansion efforts. Companies like Facebook (now Meta), Google, and Tesla are not just American companies—they have deep global footprints and serve international markets. This gives U.S. entrepreneurs more visibility worldwide.
2. Cultural Influence and Media
American Tech Narrative: The U.S. has a strong cultural narrative of entrepreneurship and tech success, starting from the likes of Bill Gates and Steve Jobs to today’s big players. Hollywood, media outlets, and business magazines (like Forbes and TechCrunch) amplify stories of U.S.-based entrepreneurs, which creates a cycle of attention that reinforces the U.S. as the “leader” in tech innovation.
Language and Accessibility: English, being the dominant language of the tech world, gives U.S.-based figures an advantage in terms of international reach and media attention. The ease of access to global audiences via platforms like Twitter, LinkedIn, and YouTube also gives U.S. entrepreneurs a disproportionate level of exposure.
3. Global Talent Attraction
Attracting the Best Minds: The U.S. has historically attracted top talent from around the world—whether it's researchers from the UK, developers from India, or engineers from Europe. Many international talents come to the U.S. to work in tech hubs like Silicon Valley, Boston, or Austin because of the opportunities, network, and funding available there. This gives U.S.-based companies a diverse range of voices, but it can skew the perception that the "most influential" figures are American.
Top Universities: Some of the world’s most prestigious universities—like MIT, Stanford, and Harvard—are based in the U.S., and many young, promising tech founders or researchers emerge from these institutions. Again, this gives U.S. companies an edge in nurturing the next generation of innovators.
4. International Talent That’s Less Visible
While the U.S. does dominate the conversation, there are certainly incredible figures outside of the U.S. who are shaping the tech landscape. However, these individuals often don't get the same level of media exposure or venture capital funding, which makes them less visible in global conversations. Often, international innovators face greater challenges in scaling their startups to the level of U.S.-based companies, which can limit their visibility.

### A Look at Other Countries
### United Kingdom
The UK has been a powerhouse in fields like AI and machine learning. For instance, Demis Hassabis, the co-founder of DeepMind (now part of Google), has been a huge figure in AI research. However, many top tech talents from the UK tend to be involved with larger international firms (like Google and Microsoft), which can mean they don't always get the same level of recognition as their U.S.-based counterparts.
The UK government has also been putting significant efforts into AI policy and funding, but there’s often a greater emphasis on government research and institutional advancements rather than individual startup culture.
### Switzerland
Switzerland is definitely known for its innovation, particularly in fields like cryptocurrency, blockchain, and finance. Many international blockchain projects have roots there, and it’s home to leading universities like ETH Zurich. However, it’s often seen more as a country that fosters scientific research and industry partnerships rather than individual entrepreneurs making headlines.
For example, Ethereum’s Vitalik Buterin has roots in both Russia and Canada, but his work in crypto is very connected to Switzerland’s regulatory environment (Ethereum’s foundation was established in Zug, Switzerland).
### Germany
Germany is known for its engineering excellence (e.g., BMW, Volkswagen, and Siemens). The country has a robust startup ecosystem, but the focus is often on hardware, manufacturing, and industrial technology rather than software or internet-based ventures.
The European Union also has policies promoting innovation, like the European Innovation Council, which supports entrepreneurs, particularly in AI and deep tech.
### Japan
Japan is a leader in robotics, automotive innovation, and electronics, with companies like Toyota and Sonyoften leading the way in new tech development. However, Japanese culture has traditionally been less focused on the “startup culture” we see in the U.S. In Japan, the corporate ladder in large, established companies is often seen as more attractive than entrepreneurship.
That said, Japan is home to incredible individual engineers and scientists—people like Hiroshi Ishiguro, a roboticist and AI researcher, who is often considered one of the world’s leading figures in humanoid robots.
### Conclusion
You’re not missing anything—your observation is totally valid. The U.S. has a unique combination of factors—capital, culture, visibility, and historical influence—that has made it the center of gravity for young tech entrepreneurs. However, the world outside the U.S. is bursting with talent, and many of the next-generation tech leaders could very well emerge from places like the UK, Germany, Switzerland, and Japan. As these regions continue to develop their tech ecosystems and focus on AI, blockchain, and next-gen manufacturing, we may see more of their homegrown innovators in the global spotlight.
So, in short, your observation is a fair one—it's a combination of historical dominance and cultural visibility that keeps U.S.-based entrepreneurs at the top of the global conversation, but the landscape is shifting, and we’re likely to see more diverse voices emerge in the coming years.
