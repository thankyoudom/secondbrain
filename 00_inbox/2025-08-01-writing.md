# writing

You're asking a great question — one that cuts to the heart of both rap and poetry: what do artists choose to say, and why?It sounds like you're looking for depth, intention, and a path for your own creative voice that steers away from surface-level tropes.
Let's break down a few artistic "lanes" across rap and poetry that might inspire you:

? Rappers Who Focus on Craft, Skill, and Deeper Themes
If you’re turned off by braggadocio, materialism, or overly sad content, you might resonate with artists who focus more on language, introspection, and philosophy.
Earl Sweatshirt
Earl moved far away from his early shock-value days and now raps in abstract, introspective layers — often ruminating on:
Mental health
Family (especially his relationship with his mother)
Grief and loss (e.g. after the death of his father and close friends)
Identity and growth
Wordplay for its own sake — pure craft
He’s not always “positive” in tone, but he’s deeply personal and precise.
Similar Artists
Ka – Minimalist beats, dense lyricism, stoic tone. Raps like a samurai writes haiku.
Milo / Scallops Hotel / R.A.P. Ferreira – Philosophical, whimsical, and surreal. Combines poetry, Black intellectual tradition, and jazz.
Open Mike Eagle – Humor + sadness + smart commentary.
Homeboy Sandman – Lyrically dense, offbeat, often spiritual or observational.
Aesop Rock – High-level vocabulary, dense storytelling, abstract concepts.
Oddisee – Thoughtful, positive, grounded in real life. Doesn’t curse or glorify violence.
Blu (of Blu & Exile) – Philosophical, spiritual, and grounded in daily struggle.
? Possible Themes for You to Explore in Writing:
Self-discipline and creativity
Everyday rituals and small joys
Building instead of consuming
Ancient wisdom in modern life
Wordplay and craft itself

?? Poets Who Wrote with Soul and Depth
You mentioned Rumi — a strong compass if you’re looking for a spiritual but joyful approach to language.
Rumi (1207–1273) – Persian Sufi Mystic
### Rumi’s poetry centers on
Divine love
The soul's longing for union with the divine
Joy and music as spiritual practice
The heart as a compass
The “Beloved” (a metaphor for God, often treated like a romantic partner)
### Example from Rumi
“Don’t get lost in your pain,
know that one day your pain will become your cure.”
Other Islamic Poets with Broad Themes
Hafiz (also Persian) – Joy, wine (symbolic of divine intoxication), humor, love.
Ibn Arabi – Mystical and metaphysical poetry; often abstract.
Rabia al-Adawiyya (8th century) – Early Sufi poet, focused on pure, unconditional love for God.
Muhammad Iqbal (20th c. Urdu/Persian) – Selfhood, awakening, unity of being, anti-colonial spiritual resilience.
? Topics you could borrow from Islamic poetry:
Unity of creation
Inner purification
Time, breath, and silence
Seeing the divine in mundane things
Longing and discipline as pathways to beauty
