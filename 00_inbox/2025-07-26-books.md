# books

Here are well-regarded book recommendations from Peter Thiel, Sam?Altman, and Nvidia co-founder/CEO Jensen Huang:

? Peter Thiel’s Favorite & Recommended Books
Thiel has frequently cited both fiction and non-fiction that shape his worldview:
The Lord of the Rings — His favorite in youth; admired for its heroic journey and exploration of power dynamics, alliances, and moral corruption (kevinrooke.com).
100 Plus by Sonia Arrison — Praised in 2012 for its vision of life-extension and anti-aging science (Farnam Street).
New Atlantis by Francis Bacon — A classic vision of state-sponsored science and utopian technocracy (Farnam Street).
The American Challenge by Jean-Jacques Servan-Schreiber — On the informational economy and the changing nature of power (Farnam Street).
The Great Illusion by Norman Angell — Offers a historical critique of military power vs national advantage (Note: Angell is the author, not Bacon) (Farnam Street).
The Diamond Age by Neal Stephenson — A science-fiction novel engaging with nanotech and AI possibilities (Farnam Street).
### Additional favorites (from broader lists)
The Sovereign Individual by James Dale Davidson & William Rees-Mogg
Psychopolitics & Things Hidden… by René Girard
Originals by Adam Grant
7 Powers by Hamilton Helmer
Capital in the Twenty-First Century by Thomas Piketty
The Decadent Society by Ross Douthat
Life After Google by George Gilder
The Hard Thing About Hard Things by Ben Horowitz (Venture Book Club).

? Sam Altman’s Recommended Reading
Altman often emphasizes books that span philosophy, AI, psychology, and entrepreneurship:
### Core titles he’s frequently named
The Beginning of Infinity by David Deutsch — “One of the most important books I’ve ever read,” on knowledge, progress, and boundless learning (LinkedIn).
Zero to One by Peter Thiel — Startup strategy insight on creating new value instead of competitive markets (theinfluentialbooks.com).
Thinking, Fast and Slow by Daniel Kahneman — Deep dive into cognitive biases and decision-making processes (theinfluentialbooks.com).
Man’s Search for Meaning by Viktor Frankl — Lessons on purpose, resilience, and perspective under pressure (theinfluentialbooks.com, elmure.com).
Blitzscaling by Reid Hoffman & Chris Yeh — On how to scale startups rapidly, even at the risk of turbulence (theinfluentialbooks.com).
Superintelligence by Nick Bostrom — A serious exploration of AI risk and strategic alignment (LinkedIn).
The Hard Thing About Hard Things by Ben Horowitz — A practical guide to managing adversity in building and leading tech companies (LinkedIn).
The Lean Startup by Eric Ries — Validated learning and efficient experimentation in early product development (LinkedIn).
Secrets of Sand Hill Road by Scott Kupor — Inside view of venture capital and deal-making dynamics (LinkedIn).
Winning by Jack Welch — Leadership lessons from GE’s CEO on building organizational excellence (LinkedIn).
Meditations by Marcus Aurelius — Stoic philosophy on leadership, self-discipline, and clarity in chaos (Hawkdive.com).
Range by David Epstein — Emphasizes the value of breadth over narrow specialization in complex environments (Hawkdive.com).
Deep Work by Cal Newport — Advocates for focused work amidst distraction to produce high-value outcomes (Hawkdive.com).
Others mentioned: Brave New World (Huxley), High Output Management (Andy Grove), The Republic (Plato), Dealers of Lightning, and various fiction/classics like Anna Karenina (elmure.com).

? Jensen Huang (Nvidia) – Book Influence & Biographies
While Huang hasn’t publicly listed reading recommendations, two recent biographies shed light on his leadership and tech philosophy:
The Thinking Machine by Stephen Witt
The Nvidia Way by Tae Kim
These books describe his strategic pivot from graphics to AI, the creation of CUDA, and his intense, hands-on leadership style. They also note his avoidance of speculative futurism in favor of immediate technical progress (The Guardian, Financial Times).

Here are book recommendations and influential reads from Jack Dorsey, based on interviews, social media, and curated lists:

? Top Books Jack Dorsey Has Recommended or Found Influential
Jack Dorsey has shared his favorite and most impactful books over the years, often highlighting them in interviews and via X (Twitter). Several sources list recurring titles, giving insight into his values and interests (Business Insider).
? The core five most influential books:
Tao Te Ching by Lao?Tzu — Jack has called this his most prized possession and one of the most influential books in his life (Business Insider, ETCIO.com, Thread Reader App).
The Score Takes Care of Itself by Bill Walsh (with Steve Jamison & Craig Walsh) — A leadership manual he regularly recommends to entrepreneurs; it embodies culture, discipline, and attention to detail (Inc.com).
Between the World and Me by Ta-Nehisi Coates — Highly regarded by Jack for its socio-historical and reflective narrative (Inc.com).
The Checklist Manifesto by Atul Gawande — He notes this often disappears from his desk and gives copies to employees at Twitter and Square (Business Insider).
The Yoga Sutras of Patañjali (edited by Sri Swami Satchidananda or Edwin Bryant) — A guide to yoga philosophy he keeps among his favorites and features in Square’s library (Inc.com).
