# Including Half marathon

You're right to ask. The original plan I gave assumes you have roughly 2 years of preparation, but considering the **current date** (January 2025) and your marathon goal date of **October 26, 2026**, you actually have about **1 year and 9 months** to prepare. Given that you can currently run 3 miles max, we can refine the plan to fit that timeline better while progressively building up your mileage, endurance, and speed over the next 21 months.

Half Marathon: 13.1 miles

Here’s how we can break it down more specifically:

### **Adjusted Plan for October 2026 Marathon (1 Year, 9 Months)**

#### **Phase 1: Base Building (First 6–9 Months)**
In this phase, the focus is on **building endurance** and gradually increasing your running volume. The goal is to get comfortable running 5–6 miles before moving on to higher mileage and more intense training.

**Monthly Breakdown:**
- **Month 1–3:** Gradual increase in weekly mileage (starting with 3 miles per run, working up to 5 miles)
- **Runs per week:** 3–4 runs
- **Saturday long runs:** Start with 3 miles, increase by 1 mile every 2 weeks
- **Strength training & cross-training** (bike, swim, yoga) twice a week
- **Cross-training:** Focus on low-impact exercises to prevent injury and build overall fitness
- **Month 4–6:** Start to introduce 5-6 mile runs, building up to 8-10 miles for long runs.
- **Tempo run or intervals:** Once a week
- **Long run:** Increase to 8 miles, maintaining a comfortable pace

**End of Phase 1 Goal:** By the end of this phase, you should be able to run 5–6 miles comfortably during the week and 10 miles on your long run.

#### **Phase 2: Building Strength and Endurance (Months 7–12)**
In this phase, we’ll increase your mileage more significantly and begin adding speed work.

**Monthly Breakdown:**
- **Month 7–9:**
- **Runs per week:** 4–5 runs
- **Weekly structure:**
- **Tuesday:** Tempo or interval training (3–4 miles)
- **Wednesday:** Mid-distance run (5–7 miles)
- **Thursday:** Easy recovery run (4–5 miles)
- **Saturday:** Long run (10–12 miles)
- **Sunday:** Recovery run (3–5 miles or rest)
- **Focus:** Continue building endurance while adding intensity with tempo runs and intervals.
- **Month 10–12:**
- **Long runs:** Increase your long run gradually, reaching 13–14 miles by the end of Month 12.
- **Weekly structure:**
- **Interval training** (more intense speed work) and **tempo runs** (5–6 miles) to start building pace
- **Long runs** (12–14 miles), focus on maintaining a consistent pace

**End of Phase 2 Goal:** By the end of this phase, you should be running 13–14 miles on your long runs and 5–7 miles for your mid-week runs.

#### **Phase 3: Marathon-Specific Training (Months 13–18)**
Now that you’ve built a solid base, we’ll begin transitioning into marathon-specific training, with longer runs and more race-specific workouts.

**Monthly Breakdown:**
- **Month 13–15:**
- **Weekly structure:**
- **Tuesday:** Speed work or hill sprints (4–6 miles)
- **Wednesday:** Mid-distance run (8–10 miles)
- **Thursday:** Tempo run (5–7 miles)
- **Saturday:** Long run (15–18 miles)
- **Sunday:** Easy recovery run (5–6 miles)
- **Focus:** Practice fueling during long runs, work on your marathon pace, and introduce some hill training.

- **Month 16–18:**
- **Long runs:** Build up to 20–22 miles by the end of Month 18. Peak at 20–22 miles 3–4 weeks before your taper.
- **Weekly structure:**
- **Interval training and tempo runs** to maintain speed and stamina.
- **Long runs (20–22 miles)**, focusing on marathon pace towards the end.
- **Recovery runs** and reducing mileage every 3–4 weeks to allow recovery.

**End of Phase 3 Goal:** Your long runs should peak at 20–22 miles by the end of Month 18. You’ll be used to running at or near your marathon pace for significant portions of your long runs.

#### **Phase 4: Tapering & Race Prep (Months 19–21)**
The final phase focuses on tapering your training to ensure you're well-rested and at your peak performance on race day.

**Tapering Phase (Final 4–6 Weeks):**
- **Week 1–2:** Maintain weekly mileage at around 70% of peak mileage, with some shorter tempo runs and intervals.
- **Week 3:** Reduce your weekly mileage further to 50% of peak mileage, focusing on short recovery runs and maintaining marathon pace.
- **Week 4 (Race Week):** Minimal running, focusing on rest and mental preparation for race day.

### **Additional Key Considerations**
- **Cross-Training:** 1–2 days a week, especially in the base-building phase, for injury prevention and aerobic conditioning.
- **Strength Training:** Incorporate 2 days per week (e.g., squats, lunges, core work) to support your running.
- **Recovery:** Regular stretching, foam rolling, and rest days are essential to avoid burnout or injury.

### **Total Mileage Increase:**
You’ll start from 3-mile runs and, by the final phase, you’ll be running 20–22 miles for long runs and be ready to tackle the marathon distance (26.2 miles).

This adjusted timeline gives you a little over 1.5 years to gradually build your fitness and strength for the marathon. By the end of the first year, you’ll be running comfortable half-marathon distances (13.1 miles), and in the second year, you’ll peak at marathon distance in training and be prepared for race day.
