# mix/master

Creating a professionally mixed song involves understanding the roles of various elements and their frequency ranges. Here’s a detailed breakdown of standard elements in a mix and their typical frequency requirements:

### 1. **Kick Drum**
- **Frequency Range:** 20-80 Hz (fundamental), 80-120 Hz (thump)
- **Role:** Provides the primary pulse and drives the track. The low end should be punchy but not overwhelming.

### 2. **Snare Drum**
- **Frequency Range:** 120-250 Hz (body), 2-5 kHz (crack)
- **Role:** Adds backbeat and provides clarity and punch. A good snare will cut through the mix without being harsh.

### 3. **Hi-Hats and Cymbals**
- **Frequency Range:** 5-15 kHz (sizzle and brightness)
- **Role:** Adds texture and rhythm. These should be crisp and clear but shouldn’t overpower other elements.

### 4. **Bass Guitar**
- **Frequency Range:** 40-200 Hz (fundamental), 200-400 Hz (presence)
- **Role:** Complements the kick and adds depth to the mix. Ensure it has a solid low end but also some mid-range definition to stand out.

### 5. **Electric Guitar**
- **Frequency Range:** 100-400 Hz (body), 1-4 kHz (clarity), 6-8 kHz (brightness)
- **Role:** Adds harmonic content and texture. Depending on the genre, it may vary in prominence.

### 6. **Acoustic Guitar**
- **Frequency Range:** 80-200 Hz (body), 2-4 kHz (presence), 5-10 kHz (air)
- **Role:** Adds warmth and a natural sound. Needs to sit well in the mid-range to avoid clashing with vocals.

### 7. **Vocals**
- **Frequency Range:** 100-300 Hz (fundamental), 1-4 kHz (clarity), 5-10 kHz (air)
- **Role:** The focal point of most mixes. Vocals should be clear and present, cutting through the mix without sounding harsh.

### 8. **Keys/Synths**
- **Frequency Range:** 200 Hz-5 kHz (depending on the type of sound)
- **Role:** Provides harmonic and melodic support. Ensure they complement rather than mask the vocals.

### 9. **Pads**
- **Frequency Range:** 200 Hz-10 kHz (depending on the sound)
- **Role:** Creates atmosphere and depth. Generally sits in the background, enhancing the overall sound without overpowering.

### 10. **Effects (Reverb, Delay, etc.)**
- **Frequency Range:** Depends on the effect used.
- **Role:** Adds space and depth to the mix. Use sparingly to avoid muddying the mix.

### **Mixing Considerations:**

- **EQ (Equalization):** Use to carve out space for each element. For example, cutting unnecessary low frequencies from guitars can help the kick and bass sit better in the mix.

- **Compression:** Helps to control dynamics and ensure each element has a consistent presence. For example, using a fast attack on the snare can help it punch through.

- **Panning:** Distributing elements across the stereo field can create a wider mix. Keep the bass and kick centered, while spreading out guitars and vocals for balance.

- **Level Balancing:** Ensure each element is at the appropriate volume level relative to others. This is often best achieved through careful fader adjustment during mixing.

### **Final Touches:**

- **Master Bus Processing:** Light compression and EQ on the master bus can glue the mix together, but avoid overdoing it. Aim for a balanced overall sound.

- **Reference Tracks:** Always compare your mix to professionally mixed tracks in a similar genre to ensure you're achieving a competitive sound.

By understanding these components and how they interact, you can achieve a polished, industry-standard mix.
