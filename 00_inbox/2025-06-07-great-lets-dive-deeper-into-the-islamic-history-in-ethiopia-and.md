?? Islamic History in Ethiopia
1. The First Hijrah (Migration to Abyssinia) — 615 AD
Context: Early Muslims were persecuted in Mecca. Prophet Muhammad ? advised a group of his followers to migrate to Abyssinia (modern-day Ethiopia and Eritrea).
The Negus (Al-Naja¯shi¯ / Ashama ibn Abjar):
Christian king of Aksum.
Welcomed the Muslim refugees, gave them protection and asylum.
He refused to hand them over to the Quraysh envoys.
Prophet Muhammad ? held the ?ala¯t al-Gha¯’ib (funeral prayer in absentia) when the Negus passed away — a rare gesture of respect.
? Key Point: This migration is a foundational moment in Islamic history, showing the first formal relationship between Islam and a non-Muslim state.

2. Islam’s Peaceful Spread
Islam did not spread through war in Ethiopia, but peacefully, through trade, intermarriage, and scholarship.
Coastal and inland trade routes, especially through Zeila, Harar, and the Ogaden, facilitated this.
Muslims became key players in commerce, education, and local leadership.

3. Harar — The Islamic City of Ethiopia
Known as the 4th holiest city in Islam (by some accounts).
Founded between the 7th and 10th centuries, but flourished as an Islamic center in the 16th century.
Harar Emirate: A powerful Islamic state that resisted Christian expansion.
### Home to
82 historic mosques
100+ shrines
Rich manuscript traditions in Arabic and Harari

4. Ahmad ibn Ibrahim al-Ghazi ("Ahmed Gragn") — The 16th Century Jihad
A military leader from the Muslim Sultanate of Adal.
Led a large-scale military campaign against the Ethiopian Christian Empire (Solomonic Dynasty) in the 1500s.
### Known for
His strategic warfare.
Temporary control over large parts of Ethiopia.
Eventually defeated with help from Portuguese forces allied to the Ethiopian emperor.
Note: This period was intense but does not define the broader Muslim-Christian relations, which were often marked by coexistence.

?? Role of Muslims in Modern Ethiopia
1. Population
Muslims make up around 34%–40% of Ethiopia’s population (~45 million people).
Majority Sunni Muslims, with a Sufi influence in rural areas.
### Found predominantly in
Oromo region
Somali region
Afar
Harari
Parts of Amhara and Addis Ababa
2. Social and Cultural Role
Merchants and traders: Historically and today, Muslims are active in commerce.
Scholars and educators: Islamic scholars continue to play important religious and social roles.
Festivals like Eid al-Fitr and Eid al-Adha are widely celebrated across Ethiopia.
3. Political and Civic Role
Muslims have historically been underrepresented in government and elite circles.
Since the 1991 fall of the Derg regime, there’s been more space for Islamic organizations, though some restrictions remain.
Movements have emerged seeking equal recognition and religious rights, especially regarding:
Islamic education
Construction of mosques
Equal representation in state media and governance
