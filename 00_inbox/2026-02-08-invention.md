# invention

Sam Altman has frequently discussed a mental—and sometimes literal—"Wall of Progress" that contextualizes the history of technology. For him, this list isn't just a collection of cool gadgets; it's a visualization of the exponential curve of human capability.
He often uses the Hand Axe as the starting point because it represents the "flat" part of the curve—a tool that remained virtually unchanged for nearly 1.5 million years—contrasting it with the vertical spike of the modern era.
Sam Altman’s "Invention Timeline"
While the specific items he highlights can vary by interview (notably with Lex Fridman and in his blog The Software Revolution), the core list of transformative milestones he points to includes:
1. The Hand Axe (The "Stasis" Tool)
Altman’s favorite invention to cite. It was the "Swiss Army Knife" of the Stone Age.
- Significance: It serves as the baseline for human progress. For a million years, a child would use the exact same tool their great-great-grandparent used.
2. Fishing
He credits fishing as a pivotal "biological" invention.
- Significance: It provided the consistent high-protein diet (Omega-3s) necessary for the rapid evolution and expansion of the human brain.
3. Writing
The first "external hard drive" for the human mind.
- Significance: It allowed knowledge to persist across generations without the "signal loss" of oral tradition, enabling the compounding of ideas.
4. The Agricultural Revolution
The transition from nomadic life to permanent settlements.
- Significance: This created the first "surplus" of time, allowing some humans to become specialized thinkers (scientists, priests, engineers) rather than full-time food gatherers.
5. Cannons (and Gunpowder)
A surprising entry he often includes to show how technology shifts power.
- Significance: It ended the era of the "unbeatable" castle and shifted the world toward centralized nation-states.
6. The Internal Combustion Engine & Steam Engine
The "Industrial Revolution" markers.
- Significance: These replaced human and animal muscle with machine power, leading to the first massive "decoupling" of productivity from human labor.
7. The Atomic Bomb
The moment humanity gained "god-like" power over its own extinction.
- Significance: Altman uses this as a cautionary parallel to AI—a technology so powerful that the "alignment" with human survival becomes the only thing that matters.
8. The Transistor / The Computer
The "Software Revolution" begins here.
- Significance: The move from manipulating matter to manipulating information.
9. The Internet
The "Global Brain" phase.
- Significance: It connected every human mind into a single, real-time network of information exchange.
10. Artificial General Intelligence (AGI)
The "End" of the wall (or the beginning of a new one).
- Significance: Altman views AGI as the final invention humanity needs to make, as it will be the tool that creates all subsequent inventions.
The "Wall" as a Philosophy
The reason Sam Altman keeps this timeline in mind is to combat "Normalcy Bias." He argues that because we live on the steep, vertical part of this curve, things that seem "impossible" today will be "routine" in five years.
> "A subsistence farmer from a thousand years ago would look at what many of us do and say we have fake jobs... since we have plenty of food and unimaginable luxuries." — Sam Altman
