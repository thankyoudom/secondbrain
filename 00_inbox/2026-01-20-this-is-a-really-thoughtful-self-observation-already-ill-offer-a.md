# This is a really thoughtful self-observation already. I’ll offer a…

This is a really thoughtful self-observation already. I’ll offer a framework first, then I’ll ask one clarifying question at the end that can help sharpen it.

A framework to think about this: Signal vs. Attachment vs. Environment
You’re circling three different forces that often get tangled. Separating them helps you see what’s actually evolving.
1. Signal: what your nervous system responds to
These are felt indicators, not logical ones.
### Signals you mentioned
Drawing sometimes feels natural
You can enter flow
You receive positive feedback
You’re drawn to accounts that express craft, taste, and physicality
These are not trivial. Flow + repeat attraction + external resonance usually means there is a real signal, even if the final form isn’t fully clear yet.
### Important nuance
A signal doesn’t mean “this must be my career” — it means “this mode of making is aligned with how I perceive and process.”

2. Attachment: a past identity that may or may not still fit
This is where your doubt is very mature.
“This might be a past vision I’m holding onto.”
That’s a healthy suspicion. But attachment isn’t binary (keep vs discard). It often shows up as:
Over-identifying with a label (“I’m an illustrator”)
Under-exploring adjacent forms because they threaten that label
Confusing medium with sensibility
### Key reframe
You may not be attached to illustration — you may be attached to a way of seeing and caring about form.
Illustration might have been the first container that could hold that.

3. Environment: what you’re exposed to shapes what you imagine
Social media is not neutral.
### If you mostly see
Physical objects
Images with taste and craft
Visual restraint, texture, materiality
Then of course your desire shows up visually. That doesn’t mean the only expression of your craft is drawing — it means your aesthetic intelligence is being trained visually right now.
Sometimes we mistake exposure bias for destiny.

A useful diagnostic: “If the output changed, would the pleasure remain?”
### Ask yourself this, slowly
If no one ever called me an illustrator again…
If drawing became one of several tools, not the identity…
If the same taste showed up in curation, design, objects, systems, or direction…
? Would the satisfaction still be there?
If yes ? your core craft is taste + composition + judgment, not illustration per se.
If no ? illustration itself is doing something essential for you.
Neither answer is “better.”

Another lens: Practice vs. Potential
### You said something important
“maybe this is because my craft is not where it could be if I were to keep it up”
Sometimes doubt isn’t misalignment — it’s the discomfort of being mid-curve.
### Two questions separate these
When I imagine being much better at drawing, do I feel relief or indifference?
When I imagine never drawing again, do I feel openness or grief?
Relief + grief are strong signals.

A gentle experiment (not a commitment)
Instead of deciding “am I an illustrator or not,” try a 90-day posture:
Treat drawing as a lab, not an identity
Draw only what you genuinely want, not what you think you “should”
### Track
Energy before vs after
Depth of attention
Desire to return (not discipline)
At the same time, notice what else wants to emerge around it (writing, curating, collecting, designing, directing).
What persists without force is usually the truth.

One clarifying question (answer this however you want):
When you imagine yourself five years from now, what matters more to you:
being known as someone who draws, or being known as someone with exceptional taste and sensibility — regardless of medium?
That answer will tell us where to look next.
