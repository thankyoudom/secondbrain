# color grading

It sounds like you’ve got a great eye for Color Grading. Even though Entergalactic is a stylized animation and Training Day is a gritty crime drama, they both lean heavily into a specific "warm" visual language to tell their stories.
In the film industry, there isn't just one single name for this, but rather a few overlapping categories and techniques. Here is how you can categorize those looks for your research:
1. The "Orange and Teal" (Teal and Orange) Look
This is the most famous color grading trend in modern cinema.
- The Concept: It relies on complementary colors. Skin tones naturally live in the orange/peach spectrum. To make those "pop," colorists push the shadows and backgrounds into the teal (blue-green) spectrum.
- Why it fits: Even if you only see the "orange," the contrast against cooler shadows is usually what makes it feel so vibrant.
2. The "Amber Grade" or "Warm Wash"
If a film feels consistently orange without much blue/teal contrast, it’s often called a Warm Wash or an Amber Grade.
- Training Day: This uses a "hot" grade to simulate the oppressive, sun-drenched heat of Los Angeles. It makes the environment feel dangerous and "cooked."
- Entergalactic: This uses a more "Golden Hour" or "Vibrant Warmth" approach. It’s meant to feel romantic, cozy, and dream-like—like a sunset that never ends.
3. The "Golden Hour" Aesthetic
Cinematographers love Magic Hour (the hour before sunset). In grading, this is often replicated to create a sense of nostalgia or heightened emotion. For research, look up "High-Saturation Warmth."
4. Bleach Bypass (Specific to Training Day)
While Training Day is orange, it also has a very "crunchy," high-contrast look. This is often achieved by simulating a Bleach Bypass.
- It retains the silver in the film grain, which increases contrast and desaturates some colors while making others (like those oranges and browns) feel much more "gritty" and industrial.
Key Terms for Your Research
If you want to dive deeper into how colorists achieve these looks, search for these industry terms:
- Colorist: The actual job title of the person who creates these looks (check out Stefan Sonnenfeld, a legend who worked on many "high-look" films).
- LUT (Look Up Table): These are essentially "filters" or mathematical formulas colorists use to start a specific look.
- Color Harmony: The theory of how colors like orange interact with their surroundings.
- Color Temperature: Shifting an image toward "Warm" (Yellow/Orange) vs. "Cool" (Blue).
> Pro Tip: Look up the "Color Palette" or "Film Palette" of these movies on sites like Shotdeck or Movies in Color. They break down the exact hex codes used in specific frames.
