# That’s awesome that you’re looking to dive into artist residencies…

That’s awesome that you’re looking to dive into artist residencies and build your path as both a visual artist and a DJ! They can be super rewarding in terms of growth, networking, and getting the space to focus on your craft. Let me break down the basics for both traditional art (painting) and DJing, and cover the general requirements, benefits, and costs.
What Is an Artist Residency?
Artist residencies provide a dedicated time and space for artists to work on their projects. These programs often offer artists a break from their usual environment, giving them time to create and experiment in a new place with minimal distractions. Residencies can vary a lot depending on what they're focused on (visual arts vs. music, for example), but they often include some mix of:
Living space (sometimes it’s on-site, sometimes it’s nearby)
Studio or workspace (for painting, drawing, or creating music)
Mentorship or collaboration opportunities
Public exhibitions or performances at the end of the residency
Residencies can take place anywhere from a few weeks to several months.

For Traditional Art / Painting
Requirements (2026)
Portfolio: This is usually the most important part. Most residencies will want to see a strong body of work, typically through a digital portfolio or website. They’ll look for consistency, creativity, and technical skill.
Artist Statement: Many residencies will ask you to explain what you’re working on, your influences, and what you hope to achieve during the residency.
Project Proposal: Some residencies require a project or idea for what you plan to work on during your time there.
Experience: While some residencies are open to all levels, others might require you to have a certain amount of experience in the field or have exhibited your work before.
Letters of Recommendation: This depends on the residency, but letters from mentors or art instructors can strengthen your application.
Benefits
Creative Focus: The main draw is the uninterrupted time to focus on your craft. If you’re in a place away from your usual environment, it can spark new inspiration.
Networking: Residencies often involve meeting other artists and curators, which can lead to collaborations or future opportunities.
Exhibition/Showcases: Many residencies culminate in a gallery show or event, which can be great for exposure.
Funding: Some programs offer stipends, materials, or living costs, though others may not.
Do You Have to Pay?
### It depends on the program
Paid Residencies: Some programs offer stipends or fully cover the cost of living and materials.
Free Residencies: Some residencies don’t charge a fee and may even provide some funding or access to materials.
Fee-Based Residencies: These are less common, but some residencies may require you to pay a fee to cover living expenses, materials, or space rental.

For DJing / Music Residencies
The DJ/music scene has its own type of residencies, and they may focus more on performance and production rather than the quiet, studio-based environment that visual art residencies tend to have. These are often more immersive in terms of performance and networking.
Requirements
Demo or Mix: Many music residencies, especially for DJs, will require you to submit a mix or demo of your work. This could be a 30-60 minute DJ set, or a collection of original music or remixes.
Experience: Unlike some traditional art residencies that may focus more on emerging artists, DJ residencies may lean towards artists who have experience performing in public or a following.
Project Proposal: In some cases, you'll need to present a plan for a specific music project you'd like to pursue during the residency (e.g., a new set, album, collaboration).
Community Engagement: Some residencies ask for DJs to engage with the local community or perform for the public, so your ability to connect with others might be a factor.
Benefits
Performance Opportunities: A major benefit is the chance to perform regularly, which helps you build your presence and get more comfortable with live shows.
Collaborations: You’ll often be surrounded by other musicians, which can spark new ideas and collaborations.
Access to Equipment/Studios: Many music residencies provide access to top-tier equipment, studio space, and even technical support for production.
Mentorship: Some residencies pair DJs with experienced producers or industry professionals who can guide your career.
Promotion: Some residencies will help promote your performances or even help you secure future gigs.
Do You Have to Pay?
Paid Residencies: As with visual arts residencies, some DJ residencies offer financial support (stipends or grants) and may even cover travel and accommodation.
Free Residencies: Some programs will cover all costs and give you the opportunity to focus entirely on your craft.
Fee-Based Residencies: There are a few programs that charge a fee, though they may offer access to valuable networking or equipment that could make it worthwhile.

Finding the Right Residency
Research: Look up residencies that align with your style or goals. Some focus on specific genres (like electronic music for DJs or contemporary art for painters), while others are more broad.
Application Deadlines: Be mindful of application timelines—some residencies have deadlines up to a year in advance.
Cultural Fit: Residencies often have different vibes depending on where they are. Some might be more collaborative, while others are solo-focused.
Examples of Popular Programs (for both types of artists):
### Residency for Traditional Art
The Skowhegan School of Painting & Sculpture (USA) – Focuses on visual arts, with a well-regarded program for emerging artists.
Banff Centre for Arts & Creativity (Canada) – Offers art residencies with a focus on interdisciplinary practices, including painting and sculpture.
### Residency for DJs/Musicians
Red Bull Music Academy – Offers residencies in different cities, including mentoring for producers, DJs, and musicians.
Berghain (Berlin) – While this famous nightclub isn’t a “residency” in the traditional sense, many international DJs are invited to collaborate and perform.
### Final Tips
Start early: Many residencies have a long application process, so giving yourself time to prepare a solid application is key.
Stay open to different locations: Residencies can be anywhere in the world, so look into places you might not have initially thought about.
Balance art with business: Alongside your creative work, learn about the business side (funding, networking, promotion), especially as a DJ. This can help set you up for future success.

Sure! Here’s a concise overview of general expectations and requirements for artist and DJ residencies:

### Traditional Art / Painting Residencies
### Expectations
Creative Focus: Spend the majority of time working on your art projects.
Engagement: Some residencies may expect you to participate in workshops, artist talks, or exhibitions.
Collaboration: Opportunities to interact with other artists, though not always mandatory.
Documentation: You might need to share your progress or create a final exhibition of your work.
### General Requirements
Portfolio: Strong collection of past work.
Artist Statement: Explaining your practice and project goals.
Project Proposal: Outline of what you plan to work on during the residency.
Experience: Some residencies require previous exhibitions or training.
Letters of Recommendation: May be needed for more competitive programs.

### DJ / Music Residencies
### Expectations
Performance: Regular DJ sets or performances, sometimes for a live audience.
Creation: You may need to produce new music, remixes, or prepare a set during the residency.
Networking: Collaborating with other musicians and artists.
Community Engagement: You might be expected to interact with the local music scene.
### General Requirements
Demo or Mix: A sample of your DJ work (mix or original tracks).
Experience: Prior DJing experience or a following in the music scene.
Project Proposal: A plan for what you’ll create or work on (e.g., new set or tracks).
Community Presence: Ability to engage with others in the music scene (not always required, but a plus).

### Common Points for Both
Time Commitment: Residencies often expect full-time dedication during your stay.
Public Sharing: Many programs will ask for a public-facing event or performance (exhibition, show, or performance).
Networking: Both types of residencies offer opportunities to connect with other artists or industry professionals.
