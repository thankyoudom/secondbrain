# devops

That's an interesting question! Folks who are into DevOps and cloud engineering often have a blend of hobbies that tie into their interest in systems, automation, and infrastructure, but they also tend to have diverse passions. Here are some things people in that niche might be into:
1. Home Lab Projects
Building personal servers: This could be for testing out different cloud services (e.g., running Kubernetes clusters on Raspberry Pi or custom servers).
Setting up virtualization environments: Using tools like Proxmox, VMware, or VirtualBox to simulate multi-cloud environments for testing.
Automation via Ansible, Terraform, or Docker: People love to automate as much as possible in their personal environments, setting up things like home networks, file backups, or even IoT devices.
Experimenting with Kubernetes: Setting up mini Kubernetes clusters at home, either with Raspberry Pi or virtual machines, to understand orchestration and deployment on a small scale.
2. Building Open Source Projects
DevOps/Cloud engineers often enjoy contributing to or building open-source tools that help automate processes or improve system reliability. They might work on CI/CD pipelines, monitoring solutions, or infrastructure-as-code tools.
They may also be contributors to large cloud platforms’ open-source projects (like Kubernetes, Terraform, or Helm).
3. Networking & Protocols
Home Networking: Setting up complex network setups with VLANs, firewalls, VPNs, and routing. They might experiment with routers, switches, and firewalls, and sometimes even mess around with SDN (Software-Defined Networking).
IoT devices: Setting up and experimenting with smart home devices and automating them with tools like Home Assistant or OpenHAB.
4. Security & Penetration Testing
Cloud engineers often get deep into security, exploring tools like Kali Linux, Metasploit, and experimenting with building secure cloud environments or setting up honeypots.
They might also engage in bug bounty programs or set up their own cybersecurity labs.
5. Linux/Unix Exploration
A big chunk of the DevOps and cloud world is rooted in Linux, so tinkering with various distros (Ubuntu, Fedora, CentOS, Arch, etc.) or configuring a system from scratch is a common hobby.
Many would also dive into things like Linux kernel hacking, containerization technologies (Docker, Podman), and system optimization.
6. Cloud Certifications & Challenges
A lot of DevOps engineers enjoy the process of getting new certifications from cloud providers like AWS, Google Cloud, or Azure. This may not just be for career advancement but also as a personal challenge.
They might participate in cloud challenges or capture-the-flag (CTF) security competitions where they have to solve complex problems under time pressure.
7. Tech Meetups & Communities
DevOps professionals might be into attending or organizing meetups, conferences (like KubeCon or DevOpsDays), and hackathons to network with like-minded people, share knowledge, and work on collaborative projects.
They also often frequent online communities like Stack Overflow, Reddit (r/devops, r/sysadmin), or specialized forums to help others and learn.
8. Gaming
Strategy games or anything that involves systems thinking (like SimCity, Civilization, or StarCraft) might appeal, as it requires planning, managing resources, and optimizing systems.
Simulation games like Factorio or Kerbal Space Program can also appeal to cloud engineers due to their focus on systems optimization, automation, and problem-solving.
9. Data & Analytics
Some might venture into data science, especially if they’re interested in working with the infrastructure behind large-scale data systems (Hadoop, Spark, etc.).
They could build personal data pipelines, explore cloud-native data storage services (S3, GCS, Azure Blob), or work on MLOps (machine learning operations) to bridge the gap between data science and production environments.
10. Building Personal Websites/Blogs/Content Creation
A lot of cloud engineers like documenting their journeys, thoughts, or learnings. This could be through personal blogs, tech tutorials, YouTube channels, or contributing to technical blogs.
They might experiment with static site generators (like Hugo or Jekyll), integrate CI/CD pipelines for auto-deploying content, and host their sites on cloud platforms like AWS, Netlify, or DigitalOcean.
11. Tech Photography / Video Creation
Some might explore the creative side and get into tech photography (capturing servers, hardware setups, or event photography) or even making videos on YouTube to teach others about cloud engineering, DevOps tools, or cloud architecture.
12. Automation & AI Projects
Building home assistants with AI (like using Raspberry Pi with voice recognition or integrating AI bots with cloud services).
Automating workflows at home, in work environments, or even for hobbies. This could include integrating chatbots, building personalized assistants, or using serverless functions for small but useful tasks.
13. 3D Printing
This is surprisingly common, especially with the trend of building custom hardware setups. Cloud engineers may design and print custom parts for their servers, racks, or even Raspberry Pi projects.

The key here is that DevOps and cloud engineers tend to gravitate toward activities that involve problem-solving, automation, building scalable systems, and optimizing processes, which makes their hobbies very tech-centric but also allows room for creativity. They might also love tinkering with new tools and technologies, learning, and pushing the limits of what’s possible with cloud resources or open-source platforms.

Books

If you're looking for books that delve into the philosophical side of DevOps and systems thinking, rather than focusing purely on technicalities, you're in for some really thought-provoking reads. Here are some titles that DevOps engineers and those in related fields often gravitate toward, emphasizing mindset, systems thinking, and the cultural aspects of work:
DevOps and Systems Thinking (Philosophical Side)
"The Phoenix Project: A Novel About IT, DevOps, and Helping Your Business Win" by Gene Kim, Kevin Behr, and George Spafford
Though it’s a novel, The Phoenix Project is frequently regarded as a foundational book on DevOps philosophy. It explores the journey of transforming an organization’s culture through the lens of IT and DevOps practices. It's not just about the tools, but about shifting the mindset towards collaboration, efficiency, and continuous improvement.
"The DevOps Handbook: How to Create World-Class Agility, Reliability, & Security in Technology Organizations" by Gene Kim, Patrick Debois, John Willis, and Jez Humble
While more technical, the DevOps Handbook also discusses the principles and philosophies behind DevOps practices. It’s highly recommended for those wanting to understand the broader mindset shifts in IT organizations.
"Team Geek: A Software Developer's Guide to Working Well with Others" by Ben Collins-Sussman
Team Geek isn’t purely about DevOps, but it is centered on the people side of technology work, which is a major part of DevOps. This book talks about how collaboration, leadership, and culture impact a team’s effectiveness, aligning perfectly with DevOps’ focus on cross-functional collaboration.
"Accelerate: The Science of Lean Software and DevOps: Building and Scaling High Performing Technology Organizations" by Nicole Forsgren, Jez Humble, and Gene Kim
This book combines research and data with an emphasis on high-performance organizational practices, blending technical and cultural elements. The philosophical underpinning is a focus on creating systems that foster innovation and collaboration.
"The Lean Startup: How Today's Entrepreneurs Use Continuous Innovation to Create Radically Successful Businesses" by Eric Ries
This book isn’t strictly DevOps but touches heavily on systems thinking. It emphasizes the iterative nature of work, feedback loops, and the idea of minimizing waste, all concepts that align well with DevOps practices and philosophy.
Systems Thinking and Related Concepts
"Thinking in Systems: A Primer" by Donella Meadows
A must-read for anyone in the field of systems thinking. Meadows presents systems thinking in a way that is accessible, touching on the importance of feedback loops, interconnectedness, and understanding complexity. DevOps engineers often draw on these principles to approach their work with a more holistic view.
"The Fifth Discipline: The Art & Practice of The Learning Organization" by Peter M. Senge
This is a classic in systems thinking. Senge’s book explores how organizations can develop a culture of learning, continuous improvement, and adaptability—core principles that align strongly with DevOps and Agile philosophies.
"Systems Thinking for Social Change" by David Peter Stroh
If you're looking for a book that extends the concepts of systems thinking into social change, this one is a great read. It helps you understand how complex systems evolve and how small changes can have big impacts—relevant to any system you might manage, whether in tech or in broader organizational systems.
"The Art of Thinking Clearly" by Rolf Dobelli
This book dives into cognitive biases and logical fallacies, helping you understand how these mental models can affect decision-making processes. While not directly related to DevOps, it’s a useful book for anyone working in complex systems, as it helps you recognize and avoid pitfalls in thought patterns.
"Antifragile: Things That Gain from Disorder" by Nassim Nicholas Taleb
This one ties in with both systems thinking and DevOps philosophy. Taleb’s idea of antifragility—the concept that some things become stronger when exposed to stress—speaks directly to DevOps, where systems are often designed to be resilient and evolve through feedback and failure.
Organizational Philosophy & Culture
"Reinventing Organizations: A Guide to Creating Organizations Inspired by the Next Stage of Human Consciousness" by Frederic Laloux
While not specifically about DevOps, Reinventing Organizations examines new paradigms for organizational structure and culture, including self-management, wholeness, and evolutionary purpose. This resonates deeply with DevOps’ emphasis on team autonomy and self-organizing teams.
"The Innovator's Dilemma: When New Technologies Cause Great Firms to Fail" by Clayton Christensen
This is a business classic, but it provides important insight into how organizations think about innovation and disruptive technologies. It's relevant because DevOps is partly about adapting quickly and staying ahead of the curve—qualities that can be found in companies that manage disruption effectively.
"Drive: The Surprising Truth About What Motivates Us" by Daniel H. Pink
This book looks at motivation in the workplace, challenging traditional views on rewards and punishment. It connects to DevOps in terms of fostering autonomy, mastery, and purpose within teams—critical components for a healthy DevOps culture.
"Work Rules!: Insights from Inside Google That Will Transform How You Live and Lead" by Laszlo Bock
Written by Google’s former SVP of People Operations, this book explores how Google’s organizational culture has been built around data and human psychology. Many of the ideas overlap with the cultural foundations of DevOps.
"The Lean Mindset: Ask the Right Questions" by Mary and Tom Poppendieck
This one is about creating lean systems and processes but goes deeper into the philosophical underpinnings of lean thinking, which shares many principles with DevOps. The book offers a mindset of continuous improvement, experimentation, and reducing inefficiencies, which are key to DevOps success.
Adjacent to DevOps: Philosophy of Technology
"The Second Machine Age: Work, Progress, and Prosperity in a Time of Brilliant Technologies" by Erik Brynjolfsson and Andrew McAfee
This book explores the impact of digital technologies on work and society, highlighting the philosophical and societal shifts brought on by technological innovation. It’s not directly about DevOps but offers insights into the bigger picture of how technology is shaping the future of work.
These books, while varying in their focus areas, all encourage a broader, systemic, and often philosophical approach to technology, culture, and organizational change. Many of the ideas in these books are directly applicable to DevOps, especially when thinking about fostering high-performance teams, creating resilient systems, and encouraging a culture of continuous improvement.
