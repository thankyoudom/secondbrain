# Yes, people definitely end up working for the United Nations (UN) later…

Yes, people definitely end up working for the United Nations (UN) later in life, including around the age of 30. The UN has a diverse workforce, and the pathways to working there vary widely, so it’s not unusual for someone in their 30s to transition into a UN position, especially if they have relevant experience, skills, or academic qualifications.
There are several ways people around that age find themselves at the UN:
1. Entry-level positions
While the UN does offer some entry-level programs, like the Young Professionals Program (YPP), which is aimed at people under 32, there are also positions that someone with a few years of experience (say in their late 20s or 30s) can apply for. These positions are often at the P-1 or P-2 level, which is typically for those with a master's degree and some work experience.
2. Mid-career professionals
If you’ve spent time working in areas related to international development, human rights, peacekeeping, or humanitarian aid, you might be eligible for a mid-level position. People around 30 who’ve gained significant experience in fields like NGOs, diplomacy, public health, law, or the private sector can make a good fit for positions in the UN. The UN looks for expertise in many different areas, so if you've built up expertise in, for example, gender equality, climate change, or conflict resolution, your background could be highly relevant.
3. Consultancies or temporary roles
Sometimes people break into the UN system by taking on short-term consultancies or temporary positions. These roles can be a stepping stone to a longer-term position. A lot of people in their 30s transition into UN work after doing consultancy work for international NGOs, governments, or the private sector.
4. UN Volunteers
The UNV program is another way people get their foot in the door. It allows people to work in areas that support the UN’s mission, and volunteers can gain significant international experience that could help them transition into permanent roles later.
5. Academic and professional networks
Some people get into the UN through networking and relationships they build over the years. Attending conferences, joining international organizations, or even working for NGOs that partner with the UN are all ways to get exposure and possibly land a job with them. People in their 30s who have spent time in these spaces may be in a strong position to land a role at the UN.
Personal experiences
There are plenty of stories from professionals who’ve joined the UN after 30. For example:
Career changers: People who worked in private sector fields like finance, marketing, or technology may pivot into UN roles focused on those sectors (e.g., technology for development, data analysis for peacekeeping).
NGO workers: Many UN staff started out working for NGOs or in the field of humanitarian aid or human rights. After accumulating relevant experience, they moved to UN roles.
Academics: People with advanced degrees in international relations, law, environmental science, or public policy sometimes transition into UN positions in their 30s, often after spending time in academia or research.
So, if you’re in your 30s and looking to move into the UN system, there’s definitely a path. Building a combination of relevant experience, skills, and networks in your 20s and early 30s can be a strong foundation for a UN career. It may also help to start by volunteering or working on smaller, related projects to build experience.
Have you been thinking about a career at the UN? What interests you about it?

If you’re coming from a cloud engineering background, there are definitely ways to translate your skills into roles within the UN system or related international organizations. While cloud engineering might not be a typical "core" function of the UN, tech roles in general—especially those dealing with infrastructure, systems, and data management—are in increasing demand. The UN and its affiliated agencies often rely on modern technology for project management, data analytics, communication, and delivery of services globally.
Here’s a breakdown of areas and adjacent opportunities to look out for:
1. IT and Technology Positions at the UN
The UN has several specialized agencies and departments that need cloud engineers, infrastructure experts, data scientists, and cybersecurity specialists. These roles could be within agencies like:
UNDP (United Nations Development Programme): Often involved in large-scale development projects, including tech-based initiatives.
OCHA (Office for the Coordination of Humanitarian Affairs): OCHA uses technology to coordinate disaster relief and humanitarian assistance. Cloud infrastructure for data management and real-time communication during crises is a key need.
UNICEF: Works on tech-based solutions for education, child welfare, and health. They need professionals to support the tech infrastructure that allows them to deliver services effectively across countries.
WFP (World Food Programme): Implements technology solutions in their global food distribution efforts, including cloud-based platforms for monitoring and data analysis.
2. Data Science and Analytics
Cloud engineering expertise is highly relevant in data science roles, where the UN needs professionals to build data models, analyze large datasets, and implement machine learning or AI tools for a range of purposes—from monitoring climate change to tracking food security.
Data engineering roles often involve working with big data systems in the cloud to ensure that the data infrastructure can scale across countries and projects.
Monitoring and evaluation positions often need cloud infrastructure skills to manage and track impact assessment data.
3. Cybersecurity
Given the scale and scope of the UN's operations, cybersecurity is a critical area. Cloud engineers can transition into positions that involve securing global networks, sensitive data, and digital services. The UN, particularly through agencies like ITU (International Telecommunication Union), is constantly looking for professionals to protect their systems and ensure privacy and integrity of their work.
4. Digital Transformation Roles
The UN has been undergoing a digital transformation, and your cloud engineering background could position you for roles that focus on modernizing internal systems, improving communication platforms, or developing cloud-based solutions to support the UN's mission. These roles could include:
Cloud architect: Helping to design cloud infrastructure that supports various UN initiatives.
DevOps/CloudOps: Managing and automating deployment of services and systems at scale.
Technical project management: Overseeing the integration of cloud technologies into the UN’s work across various fields.
5. Innovative Tech and Startups in the UN Ecosystem
The UN works closely with tech startups and innovation hubs, particularly in the realm of fintech, proptech, agritech, and govtech. Many of these initiatives require cloud infrastructure and engineering support, especially as the UN explores blockchain or AI-driven projects.
### For example
UN Global Pulse is an initiative focused on harnessing big data, cloud computing, and AI to promote sustainable development goals (SDGs).
The UN Global Innovation Hub often collaborates with private-sector tech firms and may have partnerships that could benefit from cloud engineering skills.
6. UN Contractors and Consultancies
If you’re not yet ready for a full-time position or want to get a foot in the door, consultancy work is a great option. The UN hires contractors for specific projects, particularly in areas like:
Cloud migrations: Moving legacy systems to the cloud.
Infrastructure optimization: Ensuring that cloud systems and applications are scalable and efficient for global projects.
Data infrastructure: Implementing cloud-based solutions for data storage, processing, and analysis.
7. Adjacent Fields
You may also want to consider other positions that could leverage your cloud skills, even if they’re not directly related to engineering:
Project management roles in technology-focused projects (especially if you’re familiar with managing cloud-based projects).
Technology strategy: Advising UN agencies or NGOs on best practices for cloud adoption and the digital tools that could support their operations.
Capacity-building roles: Educating local governments or organizations in the Global South on how to build and scale cloud infrastructure to improve their services.

How to Position Yourself
Understand UN’s tech needs: The UN is increasingly looking for digital transformation experts, cloud architects, and data engineers. Stay on top of the specific technologies that the UN is using (e.g., AWS, Google Cloud, Microsoft Azure, etc.).
Networking: Attend relevant conferences and webinars focused on technology for development, cloud computing, and digital governance. These are often where the UN and other international organizations scout talent.
Certifications & Skills: Make sure you have the right certifications that can align with the UN’s needs. Cloud-related certifications like AWS Certified Solutions Architect or Google Cloud Professional Cloud Architectwould be valuable. Familiarity with DevOps, Agile methodologies, or cybersecurity can also be a plus.
Look for Internships and Fellowships: The UN offers internships and fellowships which, while often not directly in cloud engineering, might give you exposure to larger tech initiatives within the organization. These can help you bridge the gap into full-time roles.
Build a portfolio: If you’ve worked on significant projects or have built scalable cloud infrastructures, having a portfolio (or even case studies) that demonstrates your experience could be invaluable in a competitive field.

Cloud engineering is an increasingly important skill for global organizations, including the UN. If you’re looking for adjacent opportunities, there’s a lot of demand for skilled professionals to help modernize infrastructure and leverage cloud-based solutions to achieve international goals like the Sustainable Development Goals (SDGs). Whether it’s within data science, cybersecurity, or cloud architecture, your skills can definitely be a great asset to the UN’s mission.
Have you worked on any international or large-scale tech projects already? That could give you a strong starting point for these roles!
