# Yes — there are Amharic-language scholars and works that engage the…

Yes — there are Amharic-language scholars and works that engage the ancient North African / Horn of Africa context (e.g., the Kush/Cush topic), though I did not find a well-documented Amharic scholar who has published deep research on the Benin Empire (in what is now Nigeria) specifically. Here’s what I found, and how you might go further.

? What I found
The book Kush/Cush and Cushitic (English title) by Girma A. Demeke is an Amharic work (or at least has strong Amharic context) that explores the Kingdom of Kush (“Kush/Cush”) and the historical-linguistic issue of “Cushitic” peoples. (Africa World Press)
It discusses how the term Cush/Cushitic is used in Biblical, historical, and linguistic contexts within Ethiopia. (Africa World Press)
It therefore shows that Ethiopian/Amharic intellectuals are writing about the broader ancient African world (at least regarding Kush) in their language.
A newspaper / media article in Amharic titled “????? ???? ??? (???-???? ???)” (“Politics, Language and History: the Kush-Cushitic issue”) appears on SBS Amharic. It likewise explores Kush and related topics. (SBS)
The scholar Aleqa Tayye (also spelled Taye) in Ethiopia is noted for having written Amharic works on the origins of the Ethiopian peoples and used traditional knowledge about the ancient Aksumite / Ethiopian origin stories. Although his focus is more on Ethiopia’s internal traditions than on Benin or pan-African empires.

?? What isn’t clearly present (in my search)
I did not find a clearly identified Amharic scholar (in readily accessible sources) who has published a specialized monograph on the Benin Empire (or the Kingdom of Benin in West Africa) in Amharic.
The connection between Ethiopian/Amharic scholarship and West African ancient empires seems under-documented (at least in the sources I found).
Many works focus on Ethiopia, its ancient kingdoms (Aksum, etc.), and neighbouring Nubia/Kush rather than the full pan-African ancient empire context.
