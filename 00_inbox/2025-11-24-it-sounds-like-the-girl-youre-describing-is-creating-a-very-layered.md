You’re hitting on something really interesting here. There’s definitely a shift in the cultural and aesthetic landscape, especially with newer art and design trends where the emphasis isn’t so much on "polished perfection" or high technical quality, but rather on rawness, imperfection, and authenticity—even if that authenticity is highly curated or artificial.
A few different aesthetics come to mind when thinking about this move away from clean, over-engineered work:
1. Lo-Fi & Raw Aesthetic
Glitch Art & Digital Decay: In the digital realm, "glitch art" has become a big thing, where imperfections and errors in the code or pixels themselves are celebrated. The more "broken" something looks, the more "real" or honest it can feel. It's almost like rejecting the sterile, smooth aesthetics of the past, and saying, "Here’s something human-made, flawed, and genuine."
DIY Culture: There's a resurgence of DIY and hand-made aesthetics in both art and design. Think about how people on social media are now embracing rough, home-made qualities in their content—shaky camera work, pixelated images, grainy video. It’s like people are saying, “I don’t need to be perfect, I just need to be me,” which contrasts with the hyper-polished, commercial style that used to dominate platforms like Instagram.
Lo-Fi Music & Visuals: The lo-fi music scene ties into this, with its intentionally "unfinished" sound and aesthetics. It rejects the perfection of modern studio production, instead leaning into imperfections like background noise or distorted vocals, creating a more relatable, human feel.
2. Anti-Aesthetic or Ugly Aesthetic
This might sound odd, but there’s been a deliberate turn toward embracing the "ugly" or awkward. People are getting tired of the hyper-glossy, Instagrammable content that looks like a brand or a commercial. Instead, we see a kind of celebration of the unpolished, like how some younger creators (especially on TikTok) intentionally post content with bad lighting, awkward angles, and imperfect framing.
Think of "bad taste" as an aesthetic—there's a kind of irony and subversion in taking something that's typically considered "ugly" or "low quality" and making it feel intentional, even meaningful. This has roots in Dadaism, punk, and grunge—where imperfection and rebellion against the norm become central to the message.
3. Post-Internet & Hyperreal Aesthetics
The Post-Internet aesthetic is one where everything feels a bit detached from traditional "realism" or authenticity because it’s filtered through the internet, digital tools, and meme culture. It’s very meta in the sense that it reflects the way we experience the world now, constantly mediated by screens, social media, and digital manipulation. A lot of the work in this space feels like it rejects traditional forms of beauty or slick design in favor of something more fragmented or strange.
Hyperreality is a key concept here—where things are intentionally designed to look "too perfect" or "too unreal," reflecting the way digital environments blur the lines between what’s real and what’s fake. The unsettling part of this is that we’re constantly consuming content that feels like it's "constructed" rather than genuinely crafted.
4. Maximalism Over Minimalism
For a while, minimalism was the dominant aesthetic in everything from design to architecture to fashion—clean lines, simple color palettes, muted tones, etc. But recently, we’ve seen a return to maximalism—bold, chaotic visuals with a heavy emphasis on texture, color, and layers. This aesthetic might embrace clutter and visual overload, but there’s a purpose behind it. The beauty lies in the complexity and richness of detail, even if it feels overwhelming at times.
This aligns with a cultural moment where people are tired of the curated, oversimplified visuals that demand nothing of the viewer. Maximalism says, “Let's throw everything in, and make meaning from the mess.” It's about embracing abundance rather than a sterile, minimalist approach.
5. Vaporwave & Aesthetic Nostalgia
Vaporwave, a genre and visual style that emerged in the early 2010s, plays on nostalgia, consumer culture, and digital detritus from the late 80s and early 90s. The aesthetic often involves crumbling CGI, retro-futuristic imagery, neon colors, and glitchy textures—things that look like they're from a bygone era but are intentionally warped, exaggerated, or distorted.
Vaporwave embodies the irony of excessive consumption while also tapping into a sense of longing or melancholy for a time that never really existed. It revels in the artificiality of the past while rejecting the pristine aesthetics of high-end, commercialized imagery.
6. Memes as Art
The meme aesthetic is another example of this shift away from high production value. Memes, by nature, are often quick, imperfect, and unpolished, and yet they carry meaning and depth through their ability to express complex social commentary or satire in a digestible way. The meme culture rejects the overly engineered, slick visuals of traditional media, embracing instead spontaneity and randomness, often to highlight absurdity or critique.
This ties into "trash art" or "low-fi" aesthetics, where things that are considered “low quality” (like JPEG artifacts, distorted images, and hastily made graphics) are intentionally used to make a statement. It's like the rawness and in-your-face quality of memes and low-budget internet videos are seen as more honest, more authentic, and more direct than the over-polished, commercialized content that feels out of touch.
7. Anti-Curatorial Aesthetic
Over-curation has been a big theme in art and design for a while now, especially with platforms like Instagram and Pinterest encouraging highly curated, picture-perfect visuals. But in response to that, there’s been a push toward a more "anti-curatorial" approach—where things are intentionally messy, unrefined, and unsophisticated. Think about glitchy YouTube videos, poorly cropped Instagram photos, or social media posts that intentionally avoid looking professional. The lack of polish in these works can feel more authentic, more real, and often more humanthan something that’s highly curated.

### Why It Works
This new wave of aesthetics, especially the ones that prioritize rawness and imperfection, are resonating in part because they’re relatable and authentic in ways that overly engineered, hyper-polished content often isn’t. With the constant oversaturation of perfect, commercialized media, people are craving content that feels more genuine or unfiltered, even if that means embracing chaos or clutter. There's a sense of rebellion against the need to be perfect, which ties into larger cultural moments about rejecting corporate control, individualism, and even the idealized representations of ourselves on social media.
People are tired of the over-engineered, too-perfect things—they want something that feels raw, real, and imperfect. It’s a rebellion against the curated lifestyle that's been pushed by social media platforms, where everything is staged and designed to look flawless. The new wave is about letting go of that control, embracing the messiness of life, and finding beauty in the chaos.

Collaborate across disciplines: Pair up with other creators (visual artists, producers, writers). The friction of different perspectives can spark unexpected ideas.

Weirdcore
Weirdcore is all about inducing a subtle kind of existential unease — familiar spaces (school hallways, shopping malls, institutional architecture) but misaligned, distorted, with creepy or cryptic text overlays. (Sayart)
The aesthetic evokes memory and nostalgia, but in a fractured way — like a dream that’s just slightly off. (Sayart)
It leans into anti-design: things are deliberately misaligned, low-res, and “wrong” in a way that feels emotionally resonant. (Sayart)
Corecore
Corecore is a meta-aesthetic. It very consciously plays with the explosion of micro-aesthetics (“-cores”) by being chaotic, rapidly edited, and emotionally dense. (Wikipedia)
TikToks under this aesthetic often mash together bizarre memes, intense music, and rapid-fire visuals — not to make a polished narrative, but to evoke a feeling or a “rush” of digital overload. (Wikipedia)
It’s almost like a commentary on how we experience the internet itself — fragmented, fast, emotionally raw.
Vaporwave and Neo-Nostalgia
Vaporwave is one of the older internet aesthetics — slowed-down 80s/90s music, nostalgic consumer imagery, corporate logos, and glitchy visuals. (Wikipedia)
It’s ironic and critical: on one hand, it’s a love letter to retro consumerism; on the other, it’s a critique of it, highlighting how meaningless or hollow that capitalist nostalgia can feel. (vaporwaveboutique.com)

This isn’t just laziness: it’s a shift — people are leaning into authenticity, or at least the appearance of it, by embracing unpolished content.
