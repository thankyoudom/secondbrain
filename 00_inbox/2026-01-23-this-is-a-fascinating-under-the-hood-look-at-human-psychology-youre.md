# This is a fascinating "under the hood" look at human psychology. You’re…

This is a fascinating "under the hood" look at human psychology. You’re touching on a topic that is often considered "taboo" in polite conversation: the deliberate cultivation of social power and the "training" of one's presence.
Many people treat social dynamics like a gym—they use specific interactions to flex certain psychological muscles. Let's break down how different schools of thought view this "unspoken" practice.
1. Stoicism: The Inner Citadel and the "Soot" of Others
The Stoics were very vocal about the "you are who you surround yourself with" concept. They didn't view it as "practicing dominance," but rather as protecting your character.
- Epictetus: He famously warned that just as a person who walks in the sun gets a tan, a person who associates with "dirty" (unvirtuous or chaotic) people will eventually get "soot" on them.
- The Training: For a Stoic, "practicing" in a contrary environment isn't about dominating others; it’s about Internal Sovereignty. If you are around loud, aggressive people, your "training" is to remain like a rock in a crashing sea—unmoved and calm. The "dominance" here is over yourself, which the Stoics believed was the only true power.
2. Carl Jung: The Shadow and the "Teeth"
Jung’s work on Shadow Integration is perhaps the most relevant to your point about practicing dominance.
- The Integrated Shadow: Jung believed that if you are incapable of being "dangerous" or assertive, you aren't "good"—you're just harmless. To be a complete human, you must integrate your Shadow, which includes your capacity for aggression and dominance.
- The Environment: If you surround yourself with people who walk all over you, your Shadow may react by "practicing" resentment. Jungian training involves realizing your capacity for dominance so that you don't have to use it. As the saying goes: "It is better to be a warrior in a garden than a gardener in a war."
- Projection: Often, if we feel the need to "practice dominance," it’s because we are projecting our own repressed power onto others.
3. Friedrich Nietzsche: The Will to Power
If you’re looking for the philosopher who most directly addresses the "unspoken" practice of dominance, it’s Nietzsche.
- Master vs. Slave Morality: Nietzsche argued that "Slave Morality" prizes humility and kindness because the weak use these traits to protect themselves. "Master Morality" prizes strength, noble self-assertion, and the Will to Power.
- The Social Gym: Nietzsche would see your "social training" as the natural process of an individual trying to become the Übermensch (Overman). In this view, you don't just "surround yourself" with people; you navigate them to test your own limits and refine your "rank" in the world.
Comparison of Thought
| Concept | Stoicism | Jungian Psychology | Nietzscheanism |
|---|---|---|---|
| Goal | Self-Mastery (Ataraxia) | Wholeness (Individuation) | Self-Overcoming (Power) |
| Social Focus | Avoiding "soot" / contagion | Integrating the "inner predator" | Establishing your own values |
| Dominance | Mastery over your reactions | Having "teeth" but keeping them sheathed | Expressing your inherent strength |
The "Unspoken" Reality: Modern Machiavellianism
There is a modern, more cynical school of thought (often associated with Robert Greene’s 48 Laws of Power) that treats social environments as literal training grounds.
People who "practice dominance" in secret are often engaging in Social Engineering. They might:
- Intentionally enter environments where they are the "top dog" to build confidence.
- Conversely, they might seek out "contrary" or difficult people to practice emotional de-escalation or frame control. ### Why we don't talk about it
We don't talk about "practicing dominance" because our current social contract prizes egalitarianism. To admit you are practicing "power moves" is to admit you view others as tools for your own development, which can feel cold or manipulative.
However, as you’ve observed, it is a very real form of "shadow training." By acknowledging it, you stop being a victim of social dynamics and start becoming an architect of them.
