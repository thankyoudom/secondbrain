# You have an incredible eye for cinematic storytelling. By adding Robert…

You have an incredible eye for cinematic storytelling. By adding Robert Valley and Pascal Campion to your list, you’re essentially looking at the two poles of modern digital illustration: Valley is about the "swagger" of the line and extreme anatomical distortion, while Campion is about the soul of light and domestic intimacy.
If you are currently grinding on anatomy and perspective, these two are the perfect "final bosses" to study.
Why these two are your "Technical North Stars"
1. Robert Valley: The Master of "Flow" Anatomy
Valley doesn’t draw people; he draws rhythm. He uses elongated limbs and "sharp" joints to lead the viewer's eye.
- Study Tip: Look at his silhouettes. Even without detail, you know exactly what the character is feeling. For your Caribbean/West African themes, imagine the fluid movement of a dancer or the relaxed posture of someone leaning against a colorful concrete wall, but pushed to Valley's extreme proportions.
2. Pascal Campion: The Architect of Atmosphere
Campion uses perspective to make a space feel "lived in." He often uses a "one-point perspective" looking into a room to create a sense of calm and invitation.
- Study Tip: Notice how he uses windows and doorways to frame his subjects. Since you like architecture and "calm spaces," try drawing a West African "courtyard" house using his lighting techniques—warm sunlight filtering through a high opening, hitting a single person reading.
Other Artists You’ll Probably Vibe With
Since you like the Entergalactic aesthetic and Mielgo’s painterly grit, check these out:
- Peter Chan: He was a concept artist for Spider-Man: Into the Spider-Verse and Entergalactic. His work is the literal bridge between Moebius-style clarity and Mielgo-style lighting.
- Tula Lotay: Her work is incredibly romantic and features beautiful, slightly surreal line work. She has a way of drawing faces and "moments" that feels very intimate and interstellar.
- Zac Retz: If you like Pascal Campion but want something a bit more "animated" and romantic, Zac is the go-to. He specializes in those quiet, romantic beats that feel like a movie still.
Motifs to Explore: "The Cosmic Diaspora"
Blending your roots with "Interstellar Romance" gives you a very specific, untapped niche. Try these:
- Floating Markets (The Caribbean Shift): Imagine a bustling Caribbean street market, but it's floating in a nebula. Use perspective to show the stalls receding into a colorful gas cloud. Use Valley's anatomy for the vendors—long, elegant arms reaching for floating fruit.
- Bio-Luminescent Textiles: What if West African patterns weren't just dyed, but woven with fiber-optics? In a "calm space" (like a bedroom at night), the only light source could be a Kente cloth blanket glowing softly, illuminating a couple.
- The "Porch" in Space: Architecture-wise, the Caribbean "wrap-around porch" is a staple. Design a spacecraft that isn't a metal tube, but has open-air (shielded) wooden decks and shutters. This creates amazing opportunities for shadow-play and perspective.

Artists to Study
1. The Successors of Moebius
- Kilian Eng: If Moebius and a futuristic architect had a baby, it’s Kilian. His work focuses heavily on scale, architecture, and "calm spaces" within vast, alien landscapes.
- François Schuiten: A master of perspective and architecture. His series The Obscure Cities features impossible buildings that feel grounded and ancient. Studying him will level up your perspective game instantly.
2. Afrofuturism & Rooted Surrealism
- Manzel Bowman: He blends West African aesthetics with cosmic, interstellar themes. His work feels like a spiritual cousin to Entergalactic but with a deeper focus on heritage and mysticism.
- Lina Iris Viktor: She uses a lot of gold leaf and intricate patterns (very West African textile-inspired) combined with celestial motifs. Great for exploring "regal" interstellar themes.
3. The Stylized Realists (Mielgo Vibes)
- Robert Valley: He worked on Aeon Flux and Pear Cider and Cigarettes. His anatomy is heavily stylized—long limbs, sharp angles—but his perspective is rock solid. It’s that "cool, urban romance" vibe you see in Entergalactic.
- Pascal Campion: The king of "calm spaces" and "romance." He focuses on the small, quiet moments in a room. If you want to see how lighting makes a space feel intimate, look at his daily sketches.
New Motifs to Explore
Blending the Caribbean/West African experience with sci-fi architecture creates a genre often called "Tropical Futurism" or "Afro-Surrealism." Here are a few ways to push your themes:
- Tropical Brutalism: Imagine massive, Moebius-style concrete structures (Brutalist architecture) overgrown with lush Caribbean flora. The contrast between hard gray angles and organic green curves is a playground for perspective.
- Celestial Textiles: Use the geometric patterns of Kente or Bogolanfini (Mudcloth) as the literal blueprints for starships or space stations.
- The "Slow" Interstellar: Instead of high-speed dogfights, imagine a couple having coffee on a balcony overlooking a Saturn-like ring system. Focus on the architecture of leisure—how would a Caribbean porch (veranda) look on a space station?
- Ancestral Constellations: Visualizing Caribbean or West African folklore figures not as myths, but as actual nebulae or planetary systems.
